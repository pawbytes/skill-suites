# Hook Generation Techniques

## Overview

A hook is the central angle that makes someone want to attend your webinar. It's the answer to "why should I care?" — the compelling reason this topic matters to this audience right now. This framework provides techniques for generating distinct, compelling hooks.

## Hook Quality Criteria

### Strong Hooks Are:
- **Specific** — Not vague or generic
- **Relevant** — Directly tied to audience pain or desire
- **Differentiated** — Not what everyone else says
- **Defensible** — Backed by research or experience
- **Memorable** — Easy to repeat and share

### Weak Hooks:
- State the obvious
- Make big claims without support
- Could apply to any topic
- Feel like marketing speak
- Don't create curiosity

## Hook Generation Techniques

### 1. Contrarian Hook

**Concept:** Challenge conventional wisdom or common assumptions. Take the opposite position of what "everyone knows."

**Template:** "Everything you know about [topic] is wrong — here's what actually works."

**Examples:**
- "Why best practices are killing your conversion rate"
- "The 'growth mindset' trap that's holding you back"
- "More content isn't the answer — here's what is"

**When to Use:**
- Research reveals widespread misconception
- User has evidence against common advice
- Audience is sophisticated and skeptical

**Risk:** Must have substance to back it up; don't be contrarian just to be contrarian.

### 2. Data-Driven Hook

**Concept:** Lead with a surprising or compelling statistic that redefines the problem.

**Template:** "[Surprising statistic] — and what it means for [audience]."

**Examples:**
- "73% of customers leave after one bad experience — here's how to be the exception"
- "Companies using this approach see 3x higher retention"
- "The average person spends 2.5 hours daily on X — are you capturing that attention?"

**When to Use:**
- Research uncovered compelling statistics
- Numbers tell a clear story
- Audience values data and proof

**Risk:** Statistics must be credible and relevant; don't lead with weak data.

### 3. Story-Based Hook

**Concept:** Lead with a narrative — a transformation, failure, or discovery that illustrates the lesson.

**Template:** "How [someone] went from [bad state] to [good state] by doing [unexpected thing]."

**Examples:**
- "How a failing SaaS turned around in 90 days by abandoning their best features"
- "The $0 marketing strategy that built a $10M business"
- "What I learned from my biggest product launch failure"

**When to Use:**
- User has personal or client stories
- Transformation is dramatic
- Story illustrates a counterintuitive lesson

**Risk:** Story must be genuine and relevant; don't fabricate or over-dramatize.

### 4. Problem-First Hook

**Concept:** Lead with a painful, specific problem the audience recognizes immediately.

**Template:** "Why [specific problem] keeps happening — and the real fix."

**Examples:**
- "Why your emails keep landing in spam (it's not what you think)"
- "The hidden reason your team keeps missing deadlines"
- "Why 90% of CRM implementations fail — and how to be the 10%"

**When to Use:**
- Research reveals specific, common pain point
- Problem is more specific than generic
- Audience actively seeking solutions

**Risk:** Problem must be specific enough to resonate; generic problems feel like marketing.

### 5. Future-Implication Hook

**Concept:** Frame the topic around where things are heading, creating urgency.

**Template:** "Where [industry/practice] is heading — and how to prepare."

**Examples:**
- "The death of [practice] — what replaces it"
- "Why [technology] will change everything about [practice] in 3 years"
- "The skills that will be essential in [industry] by 2027"

**When to Use:**
- Research shows clear trends
- User has forward-looking insights
- Audience cares about staying ahead

**Risk:** Predictions must be grounded; avoid fear-mongering.

### 6. Secret/Insider Hook

**Concept:** Position the content as revealing what others won't tell you.

**Template:** "What [experts/competitors] won't tell you about [topic]."

**Examples:**
- "What email tools won't tell you about deliverability"
- "The agency playbook they don't share with clients"
- "What top performers do differently (that they rarely discuss)"

**When to Use:**
- User has genuine insider knowledge
- Industry has opacity or information asymmetry
- Audience feels underserved by mainstream content

**Risk:** Must deliver genuine insights; empty "secrets" feel like clickbait.

### 7. Framework/Methodology Hook

**Concept:** Present a new framework or methodology that simplifies complexity.

**Template:** "The [framework name]: A new approach to [problem]."

**Examples:**
- "The PACE Framework: A new approach to customer onboarding"
- "The 4C Model for evaluating any technology investment"
- "How to apply the 80/20 rule to your content strategy"

**When to Use:**
- User has developed their own approach
- Topic is complex and needs simplification
- Audience seeks actionable structure

**Risk:** Framework must be genuinely useful; avoid over-complicated models.

### 8. Case Study Hook

**Concept:** Lead with a specific example of success or failure.

**Template:** "How [company/person] achieved [result] — a detailed breakdown."

**Examples:**
- "How Notion built a $10B company with almost no marketing"
- "Inside Buffer's radical transparency experiment"
- "What we learned from onboarding 10,000 users"

**When to Use:**
- Strong case study exists
- Example is well-known or instructive
- Audience learns through examples

**Risk:** Case study must be relevant and detailed; surface-level cases feel hollow.

## Generating Multiple Distinct Options

### The 5-10 Hook Rule

Always generate 5-10 hook options that feel **meaningfully different**, not variations of the same idea.

**Bad example (same hook, different words):**
1. "Why email marketing fails"
2. "The problem with email marketing"
3. "What's wrong with your email strategy"

**Good example (different approaches):**
1. "The contrarian truth about email automation" (Contrarian)
2. "How one change increased our open rate by 47%" (Data-driven)
3. "What I learned from 100 failed email campaigns" (Story-based)
4. "Why your emails feel like spam (even when they're not)" (Problem-first)
5. "The future of email in an AI world" (Future-implication)
6. "The EMAIL framework for writing campaigns that convert" (Framework)

### Generation Process

1. **Review research findings** — What angles emerged?
2. **Select 3-4 techniques** — Choose techniques that fit the topic
3. **Generate 2-3 hooks per technique** — Quick drafts without overthinking
4. **Eliminate duplicates** — Remove hooks that feel the same
5. **Refine remaining options** — Sharpen language and specificity
6. **Add evaluation criteria** — Why each works, who it's for, risks

## Hook Evaluation Framework

For each hook option, assess:

| Criterion | Questions |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Specificity** | Is this concrete or could it apply to anything? |
| **Audience fit** | Does this resonate with the target audience? |
| **Differentiation** | Is this different from what others say? |
| **Defensibility** | Can we back this up with research/experience? |
| **Curiosity** | Does this make someone want to learn more? |

### Scoring

Rate each criterion 1-5:
- 20-25: Strong hook
- 15-19: Good with refinement
- Below 15: Revise or replace

## Hook Selection Guidance

When presenting options to the user:

1. **Show all options** — Don't pre-select; present the full range
2. **Include reasoning** — Why each works, what audience it fits
3. **Acknowledge risks** — What could make each hook fall flat
4. **Ask about fit** — Which matches their expertise and comfort?
5. **Consider their stories** — Which hook can they speak to most authentically?

The user's expertise should heavily influence selection. A great hook that the user can't deliver authentically will fail.
