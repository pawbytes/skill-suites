# Page CRO Reference: Landing Pages, Homepages, Pricing Pages

**Purpose:** Specific frameworks, templates, and checklists for optimizing marketing pages. Read this file when the user is auditing or improving a landing page, homepage, or pricing page.

---

## 1. Above-the-Fold Requirements

The fold is the most valuable real estate on any page. Every element above the fold must earn its place. Required components:

| Component | Purpose | Common failure |
|---|---|---|
| Headline | Core value in one sentence | Too vague, company-centric, or feature-focused |
| Subheadline | Specificity and context | Repeats the headline, adds no new information |
| Primary CTA | Single action for the visitor to take | Buried, competing with secondary CTAs |
| Trust anchor | One trust signal to reduce initial skepticism | Missing entirely, or too small to register |

Optional (add when they help, not by default):
- Hero image or product screenshot (only if it directly reinforces the value prop)
- Social proof snippet ("Trusted by 5,000+ teams" or a single headline testimonial)
- Supporting subheadlines for segmented audience types

**Above-the-fold audit checklist:**
- [ ] Can a new visitor describe what this product does after 5 seconds?
- [ ] Does the headline lead with an outcome, not a feature or category label?
- [ ] Is the primary CTA visible without scrolling on both desktop (1280px) and mobile (390px)?
- [ ] Is there at least one trust signal (logo, count, badge) visible above the fold?
- [ ] Is the page free of navigation links that could pull visitors away?

## 2. Headline Frameworks

### Pattern A: Outcome Without Pain
> "Get [desired outcome] without [common frustration or trade-off]"

Examples:
- "Get boardroom-ready reports without touching a spreadsheet"
- "Run your entire agency without juggling five different tools"

### Pattern B: Specific Result + Timeframe
> "[Specific result] in [timeframe] — or your money back"

Examples:
- "First qualified lead in 14 days or we work for free"
- "Cut onboarding time by 60% in your first month"

### Pattern C: For [Audience], finally [outcome]
> "For [specific audience], finally [outcome they have been seeking]"

Examples:
- "For growing e-commerce teams, finally: inventory management that scales with you"
- "For solo consultants, finally: a CRM that doesn't require a full-time admin"

### Pattern D: Social Proof Lead
> "[Number] [audience] use [product] to [outcome]"

Examples:
- "14,000 SaaS teams use Metabase to answer questions without waiting for data"
- "Used by the ops teams at Shopify, Figma, and Notion"

### Headline testing priority:
Test the core claim and the specificity. Start with Pattern A vs. Pattern B before testing minor word changes. Do not test button colors before testing headlines.

## 3. CTA Copy Hierarchy

Ranked from highest typical conversion to lowest. Use the highest-intent version appropriate for the offer:

| CTA Copy | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "Start Free Trial" | SaaS with free trial | Clear, no ambiguity, high intent |
| "Get Started Free" | Freemium products | Emphasizes zero cost, clear action |
| "See [Product] in Action" | Demo-led products | Lower commitment, good for considered purchases |
| "Book a Demo" | Enterprise / high-touch | Direct, honest, filters to qualified buyers |
| "Get My Free [Resource]" | Lead magnets | First-person possessive increases clicks |
| "Try [Product Name]" | Brand-strong products | Short and confident |
| "Start Building" / "Start Saving" | Outcome-verb CTAs | Replace with outcome specific to the product |
| "Sign Up" | Generic fallback | Underperforms all of the above — use only if A/B tested |
| "Learn More" | Pure awareness | Almost never use as a primary CTA — signals low commitment |
| "Submit" / "Click Here" | Never | Eliminates any sense of value |

**CTA placement rules:**
- Above the fold: always
- After the core value section: always
- After social proof section: always
- After objection-handling / FAQ: always
- Bottom of page: always (include a summary line of the value prop above it)

## 4. Social Proof: What Works and What Doesn't

### Effective social proof elements (ranked):
1. **Attributed testimonials with photos** — real name, real photo, company, specific outcome. "We cut our reporting time from 8 hours to 45 minutes in the first week." — Sarah K., VP Operations, Acme Corp.
2. **Customer logos** — 6-9 logos maximum. Use logos from companies your target audience recognizes and aspires to. Irrelevant logos do nothing.
3. **Review aggregate** — "[4.8 stars / 1,200 reviews on G2]" with a link. Platform credibility matters: use G2 or Capterra for B2B, Trustpilot for B2C.
4. **Case study callouts** — a single compelling metric from a named customer. "Acme reduced churn by 34% in 90 days."
5. **Usage metrics** — "10,000 teams" or "2.3 million reports generated." Only include metrics that are meaningfully large and demonstrably accurate.

### Social proof mistakes that hurt conversion:
- Anonymous testimonials ("— Happy Customer") — zero credibility
- Vague praise ("Great product! Love it!") without a specific outcome
- Fake-looking stock photos next to testimonials
- Testimonials that describe features rather than results
- Too many testimonials stacked without structure (social proof overload creates scrolling fatigue)
- Logos of companies target buyers would not recognize

### Placement:
- One trust signal above the fold (logo row or review badge)
- Full testimonial section after the value prop section, before objection handling
- One testimonial close to the primary CTA (conversion proximity)

## 5. Visual Hierarchy Checklist

- [ ] H1 is the largest text element on the page. Only one H1.
- [ ] Subheadline (H2 or H3) is visually distinct from body text but smaller than H1.
- [ ] Bullet points are used for lists of 3+ items (prose lists convert worse).
- [ ] Bold is used sparingly — 2-4 words maximum per paragraph to highlight the key point.
- [ ] The primary CTA button is a unique color not used elsewhere on the page.
- [ ] Section breaks use whitespace, not decorative dividers (dividers add visual noise).
- [ ] Hero image/illustration directly depicts what the product does or the outcome it delivers (not an abstract stock photo).
- [ ] No horizontal scrolling on any viewport.
- [ ] Font size: minimum 16px body, minimum 24px H2, minimum 36px H1 on desktop.

## 6. Pricing Page Specifics

Pricing pages have unique conversion dynamics. Users arrive here already interested — the job is to remove final objections and guide plan selection.

### Pricing page structure (standard high-conversion layout):
1. **Heading** — outcome-focused, not just "Pricing." Example: "Plans that grow with your team."
2. **Annual/monthly toggle** — annual saves money; default to annual to anchor higher perceived value, then let users switch to monthly.
3. **Plan cards** — 2-4 plans maximum. 3 is the classic decoy structure.
4. **Recommended plan** — visually highlight the middle or second-to-highest plan ("Most Popular" badge). This is the decoy effect applied: the highlighted plan converts best when flanked by a cheaper and a more expensive option.
5. **Feature comparison** — below the plan cards. Start with the most compelling features. Mark "Pro" or "Enterprise" features clearly.
6. **Social proof** — 1-2 testimonials specific to the value of paying vs. staying free.
7. **FAQ** — address the 5 most common pricing objections (see below).
8. **Money-back guarantee** — display prominently. Even "Try free for 14 days" functions as a guarantee.
9. **Enterprise CTA** — separate line for enterprise/custom pricing ("Need more? Talk to us.").

### Pricing FAQ must-haves:
- "Can I change plans later?" (Yes — easy)
- "What happens when my trial ends?" (No automatic charge / you choose)
- "Do you offer discounts for nonprofits / startups / students?"
- "What payment methods do you accept?"
- "Is my data safe? Do you share it?" (Trust and privacy)
- "Can I cancel anytime?" (Yes, no lock-in)

### Anchoring tactics:
- Show the annual plan price per month (e.g., "$49/month billed annually" rather than "$588/year") — monthly framing feels smaller.
- Strikethrough the monthly price next to the annual price to make savings obvious.
- Enterprise plan listed last — anchors the middle plan as reasonable.

## 7. Homepage vs. Dedicated Landing Page

| Dimension | Homepage | Dedicated Landing Page |
|---|---|---|
| Audience | Broad — multiple personas | Single persona or single traffic source |
| Navigation | Usually present | Remove or minimize |
| Goals | Multiple (explore, learn, convert) | Single conversion goal |
| Length | Medium — balanced overview | Variable — long for cold traffic, short for high-intent |
| Optimization approach | Improve hierarchy and routing | Remove all exit paths, maximize conversion |

**Homepage CRO priorities:**
- Headline must work for the broadest common-denominator visitor.
- Navigation should guide, not overwhelm. 5-7 top-level items maximum.
- Multiple CTAs are acceptable, but one must be clearly primary.
- Personalization (e.g., "For [industry]" routing links) can significantly improve homepage-to-landing-page conversion.

**Dedicated landing page rules:**
- No site-wide navigation (removes escape hatches).
- Single headline, single CTA, single goal.
- Match the page headline to the ad or email headline that sent the visitor here (message match). Mismatch is the #1 cause of landing page abandonment.
- Use query parameters or URL parameters to personalize when traffic is from specific campaigns.

## 8. Feature Page CRO

Feature pages often convert poorly because they are written for engineers, not buyers.

**Feature page rewrite framework:**
1. **Problem statement first** — "Most teams waste hours every week doing X manually."
2. **Feature as solution** — "With [Feature Name], you [outcome]."
3. **How it works** — 3-step explanation, not a specification list. Use simple language.
4. **Benefit bullets** — lead with the benefit, trail with the feature. "Save 4 hours a week (auto-generates reports on a schedule)."
5. **Screenshot or GIF** — show the feature in use with real or realistic data.
6. **Testimonial** — a customer quote specifically about this feature and the result they got.
7. **CTA** — back to primary conversion goal.

## 9. Mobile Landing Page Checklist

- [ ] Single-column layout throughout
- [ ] H1 font size 28-32px minimum (not shrunken from desktop)
- [ ] CTA button: full-width or near full-width, minimum 48px height
- [ ] CTA visible without scrolling on 390px viewport
- [ ] No hover-state-only interactions (hover doesn't exist on mobile)
- [ ] Form fields use appropriate input types (email keyboard for email field, tel keyboard for phone)
- [ ] No popups that block the full screen on mobile (Google penalizes intrusive interstitials)
- [ ] Logo row / trust signal does not require horizontal scroll
- [ ] Video: autoplay muted only, with a play button visible
- [ ] Images: WebP format, explicit dimensions set to prevent layout shift

## 10. Page CRO Quick Wins Checklist

Changes that can be made without a developer, typically within one business day:

- [ ] Rewrite headline to outcome-focus (use Pattern A or B from Section 2)
- [ ] Change CTA button copy to action verb + specific outcome
- [ ] Add "No credit card required" or "Cancel anytime" directly below the CTA button
- [ ] Add photo to testimonials (replace any anonymous or photo-less testimonials)
- [ ] Remove site navigation from standalone landing pages
- [ ] Add the number of customers or reviews to the trust signal section
- [ ] Change FAQ items to objection-phrased questions ("Will this work for small teams?" not "Team Features")
- [ ] Add a CTA repeat at the bottom of the page
- [ ] Check and fix any mobile rendering issues (zoom-required text, hidden CTAs)
- [ ] Add ALT text and explicit image dimensions to prevent layout shift

## 11. Benchmarks

| Page type | Typical conversion rate range | Top-quartile |
|---|---|---|
| Cold traffic landing page | 1-4% | 6-10% |
| Warm traffic / retargeting landing page | 5-12% | 15-25% |
| Homepage (to signup or demo) | 1-3% | 4-7% |
| Pricing page (to start trial) | 5-15% | 20-35% |
| Feature page (to primary CTA) | 1-4% | 6-10% |

Benchmarks are reference points only. A niche B2B enterprise product with a $50,000 ACV will convert at lower rates than a self-serve SaaS tool — and that is correct. Always compare against your own historical baseline, not industry averages.
