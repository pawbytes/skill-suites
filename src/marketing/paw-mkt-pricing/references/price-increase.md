# Price Increase Execution

> Price increases are a normal part of business. Done well, they strengthen customer relationships. Done poorly, they destroy trust.

---

## 1. When to Raise Prices

Indicators that pricing is too low and an increase is warranted:

- Win rate above 70-80% consistently (you are not losing enough deals on price)
- Customers express surprise at how cheap the product is
- NPS is high (above 50)—customers love the product enough to pay more
- Significant new value delivered (major features shipped since last price review)
- Market rates have risen (competitor price increases)
- Churn is low and driven by factors other than price

---

## 2. Price Increase Execution Sequence

### 2.1 Test on New Customers First (60-90 days)

Raise prices for new signups. Monitor impact on trial-to-paid conversion and MRR per signup. If conversion does not drop significantly, proceed to existing customers.

### 2.2 Grandfather a Segment

Long-tenured customers (2+ years) or high-NPS customers may be grandfathered permanently or for 12 months. Communicate as a loyalty reward.

### 2.3 Give 60-90 Days Notice

Send communication the day of announcement. Do not delay.

### 2.4 Tie the Increase to Value

Announce new features or improvements alongside the price change. Lead with what is new, not the price change.

### 2.5 Offer a Lock-In Option

Let existing customers prepay at the old price for 12 months. Generates upfront cash and softens the transition.

### 2.6 Monitor Churn Weekly

If churn spikes above 2x baseline, pause and reassess.

---

## 3. Price Increase Sizing and Frequency

- Annual increases of 3-8% (inflation-aligned) generally accepted without churn impact.
- Increases of 10-20% tied to new value are manageable with good communication.
- Restructuring pricing (new tiers, new value metric) is riskier—treat as a product launch.
- Frequency: price reviews annually. Avoid more than one significant price change per year.

## 4. Email Communication Templates

See `./references/pricing-research.md` Section 3 for full templates:
- **Template A**: Modest increase (under 20%) tied to new value—warm, confident tone
- **Template B**: Significant restructuring—more explanation, grandfather offer, longer lead time
- **Template C**: Legacy/grandfather transition notice—for customers kept at old price now transitioning

Key principles for all templates:
- Send from a named person (CEO, founder, Head of Product)—not a generic address
- Personalize with customer's first name and current plan
- Lead with new value delivered, not the price change
- Include clear FAQ section
- Offer lock-in option with link
