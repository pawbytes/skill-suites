# SOSTAC Marketing Frameworks Reference

**Purpose:** Lookup guide for the paw-mkt-sostac skill. Read selectively by phase — not linearly. Each entry covers: what it is, which SOSTAC phase it serves, how to apply it in the interview, and output format.

---

## PHASE 1 — SITUATION ANALYSIS

---

### 1. SWOT + TOWS Analysis

**What it is:** SWOT maps internal Strengths and Weaknesses against external Opportunities and Threats in a 2x2 grid. TOWS extends SWOT by crossing the quadrants to generate strategic options.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Situation

**Interview Questions:**
- Strengths: "What do you do better than anyone else? What do customers say makes you different?"
- Weaknesses: "Where do you lose deals? What slows the team down? Where is quality inconsistent?"
- Opportunities: "What trends are working in your favour? Which customer needs are underserved?"
- Threats: "Who is gaining ground on you? What could make your offer obsolete?"

**TOWS Strategic Options (generate after SWOT is filled):**
| | Opportunities (O) | Threats (T) |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengths (S)** | SO: Use strengths to exploit opportunities | ST: Use strengths to neutralise threats |
| **Weaknesses (W)** | WO: Fix weaknesses to capture opportunities | WT: Minimise weaknesses, avoid threats |

**Output:** Completed SWOT table + TOWS option table with 1-2 named strategic directions per cell.

---

### 2. PESTLE Analysis

**What it is:** Scans the macro-environment across six dimensions: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Situation

**Rapid scan for SMBs — key questions per dimension:**
- **Political:** Any regulatory changes, government policy, or trade conditions that could affect your market?
- **Economic:** How is disposable income trending for your customers? Any inflation, interest rate, or FX exposure?
- **Social:** What demographic or cultural shifts are changing buyer behaviour in your category?
- **Technological:** Which new platforms, tools, or automation are disrupting how you acquire or serve customers?
- **Legal:** GDPR/privacy, advertising regulations, sector-specific compliance — anything active or pending?
- **Environmental:** Sustainability expectations from customers or investors? Supply chain exposure?

**Flag vs. Ignore:** Flag factors that are (a) already affecting revenue or (b) likely to within 18 months. Ignore distant, speculative factors unless the business is in a regulated or resource-dependent sector.

### 3. Porter's Five Forces

**What it is:** Assesses the structural attractiveness of an industry by rating five competitive forces.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Situation

**Forces + Digital Business Assessment:**
| Force | Key Digital Questions | Rate: Low/Med/High |
|---|---|---|
| Threat of New Entrants | Low barriers to build online? VC-funded challengers entering? | |
| Supplier Power | Platform dependency (Meta, Google, Amazon)? API lock-in? | |
| Buyer Power | Low switching cost? Price comparison easy? Bulk buyers? | |
| Threat of Substitutes | Can customers solve the job with a different category entirely? | |
| Competitive Rivalry | Number of direct rivals? Price wars? Differentiation low? | |

**Output:** Force intensity rating per row + one-sentence strategic implication. High rivalry + high substitutes = must differentiate hard. High supplier power = diversify acquisition channels urgently.

### 4. TAM / SAM / SOM Market Sizing

**What it is:** Estimates addressable market at three levels — Total (TAM), Serviceable (SAM), and Obtainable (SOM) — using a bottom-up calculation.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Situation

**Bottom-up method (guide a non-financial person through this):**
1. Define the unit: "Who is one customer? What is their annual spend with you?"
2. Count realistic units: "How many of that type of customer exist in the geographies you serve?"
3. Filter to SAM: "Of those, how many actually buy this type of product/service today?"
4. Filter to SOM: "Realistically, what share could you capture in 3 years given your resources?"

**Formula:** `TAM = Unit count × avg annual value` | `SAM = TAM × addressable %` | `SOM = SAM × realistic market share %`

**Common mistakes:**
- Using top-down industry reports without filtering to actual reach
- Using a single number — always output a range (conservative / base / optimistic)
- Forgetting to anchor SOM to actual sales capacity, not just market size

**Output format:**
| Scenario | TAM | SAM | SOM | Share % |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conservative | | | | |
| Base | | | | |
| Optimistic | | | | |

### 5. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

**What it is:** Frames customer motivation as "jobs" they are hiring a product to accomplish — functional (task), emotional (feeling), and social (perception by others).

**SOSTAC Phase:** Situation (feeds into Strategy / Positioning)

**Narrative interview technique:** Ask "Walk me through the last time you [looked for / switched / hired something like this]." Listen for the trigger event, the struggle, what they tried first, and what a win looked like.

**Three job types to surface:**
- **Functional:** What task were they trying to complete?
- **Emotional:** How did they want to feel during and after?
- **Social:** How did they want to be seen by others?

**Job Map structure:** Trigger → Define goal → Prepare → Execute → Monitor → Conclude → Evaluate

**How JTBD feeds positioning:** The most important unmet functional job becomes the core claim. The emotional job becomes the tone. The social job shapes the aspiration in creative.

**What it is:** Baseline assessment of how well the business currently uses digital marketing across five dimensions (PR Smith's 5Ss).

**SOSTAC Phase:** Situation

**Scoring guide (1 = none/ad hoc, 5 = systematic/optimised):**
| Dimension | What to Assess | Score 1-5 |
|---|---|---|
| **Sell** | Online revenue, conversion rate, e-commerce capability | |
| **Serve** | Digital customer service, self-serve, response time | |
| **Speak** | Content, social, email, SEO, two-way dialogue | |

## PHASE 2 — OBJECTIVES

---

### 7. OKR Framework

**What it is:** Objectives and Key Results — one qualitative, inspiring Objective paired with 3-5 quantitative, time-bound Key Results that define what "achieved" looks like.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Objectives

**Good Objective vs. bad Objective:**
- Bad: "Improve our marketing" (no direction, no inspiration)
- Good: "Become the go-to digital brand for [target segment] in [geography] by Q4"

**Good Key Result vs. bad Key Result:**
- Bad: "Increase website traffic" (not measurable enough)
- Good: "Grow organic sessions from 8,000 to 20,000/month by 31 Dec"

**OKR vs SMART:** Use OKRs when you want cross-team alignment and stretch goals. Use SMART for operational tasks and channel-level sub-goals. They are complementary, not competing.

**Review cadence:**
- Weekly: Confidence score update (0–100%) — is this still on track?
- Monthly: Progress check — are Key Results moving?
- Quarterly: Score 0.0–1.0 — retrospective and reset

**The 0.7 convention:** A score of 0.7 is considered a success. If hitting 1.0 consistently, targets are too easy. If hitting below 0.4, targets are unrealistic or work is misaligned.

---

### 8. RACE Framework

**What it is:** A digital marketing planning model covering Reach, Act, Convert, Engage — maps the customer lifecycle to measurable stages.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Objectives

**RACE KPI Table:**
| Stage | Goal | Example Metrics |
|---|---|---|
| **Reach** | Build awareness, grow audience | Impressions, organic sessions, share of voice, follower growth |
| **Act** | Drive interaction and leads | Page depth, time on site, lead form submits, micro-conversions |
| **Convert** | Turn leads into customers | Conversion rate, revenue, ROAS, CAC, avg order value |
| **Engage** | Retain, grow, and advocate | Repeat purchase rate, NPS, CLV, churn rate, referrals |

**How to use:** Set at least one objective per RACE stage to ensure the plan is not over-indexed on one part of the funnel.

---

### 9. PR Smith's 5S Objectives

**What it is:** Five objective categories — Sell, Serve, Speak, Save, Sizzle — ensuring marketing objectives go beyond just revenue.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Objectives

| S | Objective type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Sell** | Revenue / leads / conversion | "Generate 200 SQLs/month by Q3" |
| **Serve** | Customer satisfaction / service quality | "Reduce support ticket volume by 20% via self-serve content" |
| **Speak** | Dialogue, community, share of voice | "Grow email list to 10k engaged subscribers by year-end" |
| **Save** | Efficiency, cost reduction, automation | "Automate lead nurture to save 5 hours/week of manual follow-up" |
| **Sizzle** | Brand equity, differentiation, experience | "Achieve a Net Promoter Score of 45+ by Q4" |

**Why it matters:** Plans focused only on Sell objectives neglect the customer experience and long-term brand that make Sell sustainable.

### 10. Objective Cascade Method

**What it is:** Works backwards from a business revenue goal through marketing contribution to channel-level sub-objectives.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Objectives

**Cascade logic:**
```
Business goal: £2M revenue
→ Marketing contribution: 60% = £1.2M from marketing-driven channels
→ Average deal value: £2,000 → need 600 customers
→ Close rate: 25% → need 2,400 sales-qualified leads
→ MQL-to-SQL rate: 50% → need 4,800 MQLs
→ Conversion rate from traffic: 3% → need 160,000 sessions
→ Channel split: 50% organic (80k), 30% paid (48k), 20% email/referral (32k)
```

**Common conversion benchmarks (use as starting point, validate with client data):**
- Website visitor → lead: 1–5% (B2B), 1–3% (e-commerce)
- Lead → MQL: 20–40%
- MQL → SQL: 30–50%
- SQL → close: 20–30% (B2B), varies widely in B2C

## PHASE 3 — STRATEGY

---

### 11. STP: Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

**What it is:** The core strategic sequence — divide the market into segments, choose which to pursue, then define how to position against competitors in the chosen segment.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

**Segmentation variables:**
| Type | Variables |
|---|---|
| Demographic | Age, income, gender, family stage, job title |
| Psychographic | Values, lifestyle, personality, attitudes |
| Behavioural | Usage rate, loyalty, occasion, benefit sought |
| Technographic | Device, platform, tech stack (B2B) |
| Firmographic | Company size, industry, revenue, growth stage (B2B) |
| Needs-based | Primary job, pain severity, desired outcome |

**Segment attractiveness scoring matrix:**
| Criterion | Weight | Score 1-5 | Weighted |
|---|---|---|---|
| Market size | 20% | | |
| Growth rate | 20% | | |
| Competitive intensity | 15% | | |
| Strategic fit | 20% | | |
| LTV potential | 15% | | |
| Cost to reach | 10% | | |

**Targeting models:**
- **Undifferentiated:** One offer for all (commodities, mass FMCG)
- **Differentiated:** Separate offers per segment (requires resource)
- **Concentrated/Niche:** All resources on one segment (best for SMBs and startups)
- **Micromarketing:** Hyper-local or individual personalisation (requires data maturity)

**Positioning — perceptual map:** Choose two axes that matter most to target customers (not to the brand). Plot competitors and identify the open, desirable space. Axes should come from customer research (JTBD interviews, surveys), not gut feel.

---

### 12. Moore's Positioning Statement Formula

**What it is:** A fill-in-the-blank template for writing a precise, differentiated positioning statement.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

**Formula:**
> "For [target customer], who [has this need or problem], [Brand Name] is the [market category] that [delivers this key benefit]. Unlike [primary alternative], [Brand Name] [key differentiator]."

**Workshopping with a client:** Fill each slot separately. Challenge every slot — "Is that really the primary need?" "What do customers actually call this category?" The differentiation slot must be specific and defensible.

**Weak positioning patterns to avoid:**
- "We are the best quality / most innovative / most customer-focused" — table stakes, not differentiation
- A statement so broad it could apply to three competitors
- Differentiating on a feature customers do not care about
- Using internal language instead of customer language

---

### 13. Ansoff Matrix

**What it is:** A 2x2 growth strategy matrix across existing/new markets and existing/new products.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

| | Existing Products | New Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | Market Penetration (lowest risk) | Product Development |
| **New Markets** | Market Development | Diversification (highest risk) |

**Starting point by brand stage:**
- Early-stage / pre-PMF: Penetration first — prove the core before expanding
- Growth stage: Penetration + Market Development in parallel
- Mature brand: Product Development to avoid commoditisation
- Declining brand: Diversification or exit

**Ansoff → Channel connection:** Market Penetration maps to performance marketing and CRO. Market Development maps to new geo / audience targeting. Product Development maps to content and email nurture for existing lists. Diversification requires entirely new channel strategies.

---

### 14. Porter's Generic Strategies

**What it is:** Three fundamental ways to achieve sustainable competitive advantage: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, or Focus (narrow scope with either cost or differentiation).

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

**Implications for marketing:**
| Strategy | Messaging | Channel Priority | Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cost Leadership | Price/value claims, efficiency | High-volume paid, price comparison | Functional, product-led |
| Differentiation | Uniqueness, quality, brand story | Brand, content, PR, community | Emotional, aspirational |
| Focus (Niche) | Deep expertise, specialist | Targeted paid, SEO niche terms, partnerships | Highly specific, expert-level |

**The "stuck in the middle" danger:** Trying to compete on both cost and differentiation dilutes both and leads to losing ground to specialists on either side. Strategy must make a clear choice — confirm this in the planning interview before building tactics.

---

### 15. Blue Ocean Strategy — 4 Actions Framework

**What it is:** A tool for finding uncontested market space by questioning industry assumptions — what to eliminate, reduce, raise, and create.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

**4 Actions grid:**
| Action | Question |
|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors the industry competes on should be eliminated entirely? |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should be reduced well below industry standard? |
| **Raise** | Which factors should be raised well above industry standard? |
| **Create** | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? |

**Strategy Canvas:** A line graph plotting your brand vs. competitors across 6-10 key competing factors on the X-axis, with level of offering (low to high) on Y-axis. The goal is a divergent curve — where your line departs from competitors signals your differentiation.

**When to invoke:** Use when the client operates in a highly commoditised market and competitive rivalry (from Five Forces) is rated High.

---

### 16. Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder)

**What it is:** Maps what a business offers against what customers actually need — ensuring product/service features connect directly to customer jobs, pains, and desired gains.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

**Customer Profile:**
- **Jobs:** What are they trying to accomplish? (functional, emotional, social)
- **Pains:** What frustrates them before, during, and after the job?
- **Gains:** What outcomes and benefits do they desire?

**Value Map:**
- **Products & Services:** What you offer
- **Pain Relievers:** How your offer reduces specific pains
- **Gain Creators:** How your offer produces desired gains

**"Fit":** Achieved when Pain Relievers address the most important Pains and Gain Creators deliver the most desired Gains. Rank pains/gains by severity/importance before mapping.

**Interview questions:**
- "What is the biggest frustration customers express about current solutions?"
- "What does a perfect outcome look like for the customer?"
- "What would make them look good or feel good in their situation?"

**Output template:** Two-column table — left: ranked Customer Pains/Gains; right: matching Pain Relievers/Gain Creators.

---

### 17. Online Value Proposition (OVP) — PR Smith

**What it is:** A specific articulation of why a customer should engage with the brand online — what the digital channel offers that alternatives (including offline) do not.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy

**The 4 OVP questions:**
1. Why should someone visit your website / digital channels (vs. a competitor's)?
2. Why should they stay and engage?
3. Why should they return?
4. Why should they recommend it?

**OVP vs. General Value Proposition:** The general VP applies to the brand overall. The OVP is specific to the digital experience — speed, content depth, tools, personalisation, convenience, community. A strong OVP is often the differentiating layer for a commodity product.

---

### 18. Customer Journey Mapping

**What it is:** A visual representation of every step a customer takes from problem recognition through to advocacy, mapped against their actions, thoughts, emotions, and the brand's touchpoints.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Strategy (outputs feed directly into Tactics selection)

**Journey stages to map:**
| Stage | Channel | Action | Thought | Emotion | Pain Point | Brand Response |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Trigger | | | | | | |
| Awareness | | | | | | |
| Consideration | | | | | | |
| Decision | | | | | | |
| Retention | | | | | | |
| Advocacy | | | | | | |

**McKinsey Consumer Decision Journey (non-linear):** Customers do not move linearly — they loop back (especially in consideration), are influenced by peer networks, and have trigger moments post-purchase that either deepen loyalty or cause churn. Map the key loop-back points and loyalty triggers.

**Journey → Tactics:** Each stage cell with a pain point or gap becomes a tactic requirement. "No comparison content at Consideration stage" → create comparison landing page. This is how the journey map directly drives Phase 4.

## PHASE 4 — TACTICS

---

### 19. AARRR Pirate Metrics (Dave McClure)

**What it is:** A five-stage funnel model — Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral — used to diagnose where the biggest growth lever sits.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Diagnosing the biggest leak:** Calculate conversion rate between each stage. The stage with the worst conversion relative to benchmark is the priority for tactical investment.

| Stage | Definition | Benchmark range |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition** | Visitors from any channel | Cost per visitor varies widely |
| **Activation** | First "aha" moment / meaningful action | 20–40% of new visitors |
| **Retention** | Return visits / repeat usage | Varies by model; <20% is poor |
| **Revenue** | Paying conversion | 1–5% visitor-to-pay (direct) |
| **Referral** | Advocates who bring others | NPS 40+ usually correlates |

**AARRR → tactical priority:** Fix Activation before spending more on Acquisition. Fix Retention before scaling Revenue. A leaky bucket wastes acquisition spend.

---

### 20. ICE Scoring (Sean Ellis)

**What it is:** A prioritisation framework scoring tactical ideas on Impact, Confidence, and Ease to produce a ranked backlog.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Formula:** `ICE Score = (Impact + Confidence + Ease) / 3`

**Scoring guide (1-10 each):**
- **Impact:** If this works, how much will it move the key metric?
- **Confidence:** How certain are you it will work (based on data, tests, analogues)?
- **Ease:** How quick and cheap is it to implement?

**ICE-scored tactic table template:**
| Tactic | Impact | Confidence | Ease | ICE Score | Owner | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | | | |

**How to use:** Score a backlog of 10-20 candidate tactics. Work top-down from highest ICE score. Re-score after each sprint based on new learnings.

---

### 21. PIE Framework (Goward)

**What it is:** A CRO-specific prioritisation framework scoring pages or experiments on Potential, Importance, and Ease.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Scoring (1-10 each):**
- **Potential:** How much improvement is possible? (low-converting pages score higher)
- **Importance:** How much traffic / revenue does this page influence?
- **Ease:** How technically and politically easy is it to run the test?

`PIE Score = (Potential + Importance + Ease) / 3`

**PIE vs ICE:** Use PIE when prioritising CRO experiments on specific pages. Use ICE for a broader mixed-tactic backlog that includes content, paid, email, and product changes.

---

### 22. Hub-Hero-Help Content Model

**What it is:** A three-tier content strategy that allocates content types by audience scale, frequency, and intent.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

| Tier | Description | Cadence | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | High-production, emotional, broad reach — earns brand attention | 2-4× per year | Brand film, viral campaign, major report launch |
| **Hub** | Regular serialised content that maintains audience relationship | Weekly / Bi-weekly | Podcast, video series, newsletter, webinar series |
| **Help** | Always-on, search-driven, answers specific questions | Daily / Ongoing | How-to guides, FAQs, tutorials, product comparison pages |

**Production cadence note:** Most SMBs should invest 60–70% of content effort in Help (highest search ROI), 25-30% in Hub (audience retention), and 5-10% in Hero (brand spikes). Not the reverse.

---

### 23. Pillar-Cluster SEO Model

**What it is:** A site architecture and content strategy where a broad "pillar page" covers a topic comprehensively and supporting "cluster pages" cover sub-topics, all linking back to the pillar.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Architecture:**
```
Pillar Page: "Complete Guide to [Core Topic]" (2,000–5,000 words)
  └── Cluster: "[Sub-topic 1]" — links to pillar
  └── Cluster: "[Sub-topic 2]" — links to pillar
  └── Cluster: "[Sub-topic 3]" — links to pillar
  └── Cluster: "[Sub-topic N]" — links to pillar
```

**Planning steps:** (1) Identify 3-5 core topic pillars aligned to buyer journey stages. (2) Keyword research each pillar for 10-20 cluster topic ideas. (3) Audit existing content — map to pillars or flag for deletion/consolidation. (4) Build internal linking plan.

**Note:** For detailed SEO execution, invoke the paw-mkt-seo skill.

---

### 24. TOFU / MOFU / BOFU

**What it is:** Top, Middle, and Bottom of Funnel — a content classification system aligned to where a buyer is in their decision journey.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Content format by funnel stage:**
| Stage | Buyer State | Content Formats | Channel Fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TOFU** | Problem-aware, not solution-aware | Blog posts, social content, podcasts, explainer videos, infographics | SEO, social, paid awareness |
| **MOFU** | Solution-aware, evaluating options | Case studies, webinars, comparison guides, email nurture, whitepapers | Email, retargeting, organic search |
| **BOFU** | Vendor-aware, deciding | Demos, free trials, pricing pages, testimonials, ROI calculators | Direct, branded search, sales enablement |

**Content audit vs. TOFU/MOFU/BOFU:** Categorise every existing content asset into one stage. Calculate percentage coverage per stage. Gaps reveal where the funnel leaks and where new content investment is needed.

### 25. 7P Marketing Mix

**What it is:** An expanded marketing mix for services and digital businesses covering Product, Price, Place, Promotion, People, Process, and Physical Evidence.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Digital translation of each P:**
| P | Digital Tactic |
|---|---|
| **Product** | UX, feature set, packaging, onboarding flow |
| **Price** | Pricing page design, tier structure, freemium, discount strategy |
| **Place** | Distribution channels, marketplace presence, website, app |
| **Promotion** | Paid, SEO, content, email, social, PR, partnerships |
| **People** | Support team, sales team, community management, brand voice |
| **Process** | Purchase flow, onboarding, renewal, complaint handling |
| **Physical Evidence** | Reviews, case studies, certifications, brand design, packaging |

**Tactics coverage check:** Use as a checklist — ensure tactics in the plan address all 7Ps, not just Promotion.

---

### 26. 70/20/10 Budget Rule

**What it is:** A budget allocation framework — 70% to proven core channels, 20% to emerging or scaling channels, 10% to experimental/new bets.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Tactics

**Application to channel budget:**
- **70% (Core):** Channels with proven ROI and predictable returns. Protect this budget unless core metrics decline.
- **20% (Scale):** Channels showing early positive signals — scale investment to test ceiling.
- **10% (Experiment):** New channels, creative formats, emerging platforms. Accept higher failure rate. Document learnings.

**Example allocation by brand stage:**
| Stage | Core 70% | Emerging 20% | Experimental 10% |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early growth | SEO + email | Paid social | Influencer / affiliate |
| Scaling | Paid search + SEO | Programmatic | Podcast / sponsorship |
| Mature brand | Multi-channel proven mix | CRO / personalisation | New platform / format |

## PHASE 4.5 — SITUATIONAL FRAMEWORK ROUTING

> **Purpose:** Use this section at the start of Phase 4 (Tactics) to determine which channel strategies, copywriting frameworks, and funnel emphasis are most appropriate for this brand's specific situation. Read the completed Situation, Objectives, and Strategy phases, then work through the three layers below in order. The output is a Recommended Playbook label that shapes every tactic decision that follows.

---

### Layer 1: Brand Maturity Classifier

Read from Phase 1 (Situation) — business stage, team size, revenue baseline, 5S scores, and from Phase 3 (Strategy) — Ansoff Matrix position.

| Maturity Stage | Signals | Primary Growth Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-Launch** | No paying customers, product not yet live or in beta | Proof of concept — need first customers, not scale |
| **Early-Stage** | 0–18 months live, <£/$500k ARR, <20 customers | Product-market fit — need signal on who buys and why |
| **Growth** | PMF confirmed, MoM growth >10%, scaling a repeatable model | Scaling acquisition and activation without breaking retention |
| **Scale / Established** | Predictable revenue, >1,000 customers, market-aware brand | Efficiency, retention, and category leadership |

**Decision rule:** If Ansoff position is Market Development or Diversification, treat the new segment as "Early-Stage" regardless of the brand's overall maturity — the growth constraint is the same.

---

### Layer 2: AARRR Priority Gap

Score the current state at each AARRR stage from Phase 1 data and Phase 2 OKRs. The stage with the lowest score relative to its benchmark is the **Priority Gap** and determines where tactical investment goes first.

| AARRR Stage | "Fix This First" Signal | Benchmark Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition** | Traffic is low, CAC is high, channel mix is 1 source | <1,000 monthly sessions or single-channel dependency |
| **Activation** | Traffic exists but new users don't take a meaningful first action | <20% of new visitors reach activation event |
| **Retention** | Customers churn before becoming profitable or habitual | Monthly churn >5% (SaaS) / repeat rate <20% (e-comm) |
| **Revenue** | Conversions happen but avg order value / LTV is too low | LTV:CAC ratio <3:1 |
| **Referral** | Customers are not recommending or advocating | NPS <30, referral traffic <5% of total |

**Constraint rule:** Fix Activation before scaling Acquisition. Fix Retention before optimising Revenue. A leaky funnel wastes every pound/dollar of acquisition spend — confirm the priority gap before selecting channels.

---

### Layer 3: Funnel Weight Selector

Combine Brand Maturity (Layer 1) and AARRR Priority Gap (Layer 2) to determine whether this plan should be weighted toward TOFU, MOFU, BOFU, or a balanced mix.

| Brand Maturity | Primary AARRR Gap | Recommended Funnel Weight | Playbook Name |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-Launch | Acquisition | 80% TOFU / 20% MOFU / 0% BOFU | **Awareness-First** |
| Early-Stage | Acquisition | 60% TOFU / 30% MOFU / 10% BOFU | **Demand Generation** |
| Early-Stage | Activation | 30% TOFU / 50% MOFU / 20% BOFU | **Conversion-Led** |
| Growth | Acquisition | 50% TOFU / 30% MOFU / 20% BOFU | **Scale Acquisition** |
| Growth | Retention | 20% TOFU / 40% MOFU / 40% BOFU | **Retention-Led** |
| Growth | Revenue | 20% TOFU / 30% MOFU / 50% BOFU | **Monetisation-Led** |
| Scale / Established | Referral | 30% TOFU / 20% MOFU / 20% BOFU / 30% Advocacy | **Flywheel / PLG** |
| Scale / Established | Retention | 20% TOFU / 20% MOFU / 30% BOFU / 30% Expansion | **LTV Maximisation** |
| Any | Revenue (LTV:CAC <3:1) | 10% TOFU / 20% MOFU / 70% BOFU | **Efficiency Sprint** |

### Channel-Framework Pairing by Playbook

Once the Playbook Name is determined, use the table below to assign the most relevant strategic frameworks per channel. These guide which approaches to prioritise inside each specialist skill.

#### Awareness-First Playbook (Pre-Launch, Acquisition gap)
*Goal: Build an audience before you have revenue. Establish category presence and attract early adopters.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Content / SEO | High | Pillar-Cluster (TOFU topics), Hub-Hero-Help (Hero first), StoryBrand narrative structure | Founding content pillar — "what problem does this solve?" |
| Organic Social | High | See-Think-Do-Care (See stage only), Platform-native hooks, Community seeding | Brand voice + early follower base |
| PR / Earned Media | High | Newsjacking, Thought Leadership, Data-driven story playbook | First brand mentions and backlinks |
| Email | Medium | Pre-launch waitlist sequence, curiosity + anticipation subject lines | Qualified waitlist with 30–40% open rate target |
| Paid Ads | Low — test only | Brand awareness objective (CPM), not conversion. 10% budget max. | Audience data and creative signal for later |
| Influencer | Medium | Micro/nano seeding, gifting programme, UGC collection | Social proof inventory before launch |

**Copywriting framework:** StoryBrand (Donald Miller) — position the customer as the hero, the brand as the guide. Every message answers: "What problem do you solve, for whom, and what does life look like after?"

#### Demand Generation Playbook (Early-Stage, Acquisition gap)
*Goal: Build predictable lead flow. Prove which channels deliver qualified buyers at sustainable CAC.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| SEO | High | Pillar-Cluster (TOFU + MOFU blend), Keyword intent mapping, Content gap analysis | 5–10 ranking pages driving qualified traffic |
| Content | High | Hub-Hero-Help (70% Help, 25% Hub, 5% Hero), TOFU/MOFU content map | Regular publishing cadence with lead magnet tie-in |
| Paid Search | Medium | Broad → Phrase → Exact match ladder, Smart bidding after 50 conversions, Negative keyword build | First profitable paid channel proof point |
| Paid Social | Medium | Cold audience (LAL) → Retargeting → Warm list, Creative testing (3-phase), UGC-style ads | Validated CPL and creative angles |
| Email | Medium | Welcome sequence (5–7 emails), AIDA-structured nurture, Segmentation by lead source | MQL pipeline from organic and paid leads |
| Referral | Low–Medium | Basic two-sided incentive, NPS survey trigger, Referral ask post-first-value | Early K-factor baseline |

**Copywriting framework:** AIDA (Attention–Interest–Desire–Action) for ads and landing pages. PAS (Problem–Agitate–Solution) for email nurture and long-form content. Both focus on the buyer's functional job from JTBD research.

#### Conversion-Led Playbook (Early-Stage, Activation gap)
*Goal: Fix the leaky bucket. Traffic exists but users aren't converting or activating. Improve the journey between click and first value.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| CRO / Landing Pages | High | PIE scoring, A/B test framework, Objection matrix, Jobs-to-be-Done messaging audit | Conversion rate lift on key entry pages |
| Email | High | Onboarding sequence (5–10 emails), Behaviour-triggered automation, "Aha moment" acceleration | Activation rate improvement within 7-day cohort |
| Content / MOFU | High | Comparison guides, Case studies (Challenge–Solution–Results), Demo/trial enablement content | Consideration-stage content that addresses top objections |
| Paid Retargeting | Medium | Warm audience retargeting (site visitors, email list), Sequential messaging, Objection-based creative | Re-capture of dropped-off traffic |
| Paid Search | Medium | BOFU branded + competitor terms, Price/feature extensions, Review sitelinks | High-intent capture at decision stage |
| Community | Low–Medium | In-product community touchpoint, Onboarding office hours, Power user identification | Early social proof and activation support |

**Copywriting framework:** Value Proposition Canvas messaging audit — every conversion touchpoint must directly address a ranked Customer Pain with a specific Pain Reliever. Use Moore's Positioning Statement as the headline test: does every page answer "For [who], we solve [what]"?

#### Scale Acquisition Playbook (Growth stage, Acquisition gap)
*Goal: Pour fuel on a proven model. CAC is known, activation is working — now scale reach without blowing efficiency.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paid Search | High | Performance Max with audience signals, Smart bidding at scale, Brand + non-brand split | Scaled conversion volume at stable CAC |

| PR | Medium | Data-driven studies, Tier 1 press outreach, Podcast guest circuit | Domain authority + brand credibility signals |

**Copywriting framework:** BAB (Before–After–Bridge) for scaled creative — fast-moving, outcome-focused. At scale, test multiple emotional angles (fear of missing out, social proof, aspiration) and let data determine the winner, not gut feel.

#### Retention-Led Playbook (Growth stage, Retention gap)
*Goal: Stop the churn. Acquisition is working but customers aren't staying long enough to be profitable.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Email / Automation | High | Churn prediction segmentation, Win-back sequence (3–5 emails), NPS-driven feedback loops, Milestone celebrations | Churn rate reduction, improved cohort LTV |
| Content / MOFU–BOFU | High | Customer education series, Use-case expansion content, "How to get more value" library | Feature adoption and product stickiness |
| Community | High | Discord/Slack community, Power user programme, Customer advisory board | Peer accountability and network effect |
| CRO / Onboarding | High | Onboarding flow audit, In-app messaging, First-value acceleration (shorten time to "aha moment") | Improved D7/D30 retention cohort |
| Referral | Medium | Advocacy programme (NPS promoters → referral ask), Case study recruitment | Social proof + organic growth loop |
| Paid Retargeting | Low | Win-back ads for churned cohort, Upsell retargeting to active users | Incremental revenue from existing base |

**Copywriting framework:** Jobs-to-be-Done re-engagement messaging — identify the functional job that was "hired" but not fully completed. Win-back copy should name the uncompleted job and restate the path to achieving it. Avoid generic "we miss you" messaging.

#### Monetisation-Led Playbook (Growth stage, Revenue/LTV gap)
*Goal: Increase revenue per customer. Traffic and activation are healthy but LTV:CAC ratio is below 3:1.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Email | High | Upsell/cross-sell sequences (2–4 emails), Usage-triggered upgrade prompts, Pricing tier migration campaigns | ACV/AOV increase from existing base |
| Content / BOFU | High | ROI calculators, Total cost of ownership comparisons, Upgrade case studies | Justification content for higher-tier purchase |
| CRO / Pricing Page | High | Good-Better-Best tier presentation, Decoy Effect pricing, Annual vs monthly upsell, Charm pricing audit | Pricing page conversion to higher tiers |
| Paid Retargeting | Medium | Upsell retargeting to active users by tier, Feature announcement ads, Competitor conquest | Incremental revenue from engaged audience |
| Community / Advocacy | Medium | Power user upgrade incentives, Peer benchmarking features, VIP customer programme | Premium retention and expansion revenue |
| Partnerships | Medium | Integration partnerships, Bundle pricing with complementary tools, Co-sell agreements | Revenue through partner channel |

**Copywriting framework:** Rule of 100 (pricing psychology) + Anchoring (present highest tier first). Every upgrade message should articulate the specific gain unlocked at the next tier using customer-language from JTBD interviews — not feature names.

#### Flywheel / PLG Playbook (Scale, Referral gap)
*Goal: Make the product itself the acquisition engine. Reduce paid dependency through viral and product-led loops.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Referral Programme | High | K-factor calculation, Two-sided incentive design, In-product referral triggers, Milestone-based rewards | K > 0.5 referral coefficient |
| Community | High | Community-led growth loops, User advocacy programme, Ambassador tiers (Seed/Growth/Elite) | Self-sustaining community driving organic signups |

| Email | Medium | Viral referral sequence, Shared content with network effect, User milestone announcements | Social sharing and referral triggers |

**Frameworks:** Viral coefficient / K-factor optimisation, Flywheel Effect (product → users → data → better product → more users), Network Effects identification (direct, indirect, data, social, platform).

#### LTV Maximisation Playbook (Scale / Established, Retention or Expansion gap)
*Goal: Maximise revenue from an established base. NRR (Net Revenue Retention) above 100% is the target.*

| Channel | Priority | Frameworks to Apply | Key Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| Email / Automation | High | Segmented expansion sequences, Health score-triggered outreach, QBR invitations, Renewal campaigns | NRR improvement, reduced involuntary churn |
| Community | High | Executive community (peer network), Customer success community, Customer advisory board | Deepened relationship and lock-in |
| Content / Expansion | High | Advanced use-case content, Integration guides, Industry benchmarking reports | Feature adoption across product surface area |
| CRO / Account Expansion | High | In-app expansion triggers, Seat expansion prompts, Usage alerts ("you've hit 80% of your limit") | ARPU expansion without sales friction |
| Referral / Advocacy | High | NPS promoter referral programme, G2/Capterra review campaigns, Testimonial collection | Social proof flywheel + net new acquisition |
| PR / Thought Leadership | Medium | Customer success stories, Industry awards, Data-driven category reports | Brand authority in established category |

**Frameworks:** Net Revenue Retention (NRR) = (Starting MRR + Expansion MRR − Churn MRR − Contraction MRR) / Starting MRR × 100. NRR >100% means the existing customer base grows revenue without any new customers. This is the benchmark for a healthy Scale-stage business.

### 27. RACI Matrix

**What it is:** A responsibility assignment matrix — Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns the outcome), Consulted (input required), Informed (kept updated).

**SOSTAC Phase:** Action

**RACI rules:**
- Every task must have exactly one Accountable. Multiple A's = no clear ownership.
- Not everyone needs to be Consulted — over-consulting creates bottlenecks.
- Informed roles receive outputs, not drafts.

**Template — standard campaign launch:**
| Task | Marketing Lead | Designer | Developer | Exec Sponsor | Analytics |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Campaign brief | A/R | C | C | C | I |
| Creative production | C | R | I | I | I |
| Landing page build | C | C | R | I | C |
| Campaign launch | A | I | R | I | R |
| Performance report | A/R | I | I | I | C |

---

### 28. Agile Marketing Sprints

**What it is:** Adapts the Scrum framework to marketing — 2-week sprints of planned, prioritised work with regular review and retrospective cycles.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Action

**Sprint cycle for marketing:**
1. **Sprint Planning (Day 1):** Select highest-ICE-scored items from backlog. Define sprint goal. Assign owners.
2. **Daily Standup (15 min):** What did I do? What will I do? Any blockers?
3. **Sprint Review (Day 10/14):** Demo outputs. Measure results vs. sprint goal.
4. **Retrospective (30 min):** What worked / what didn't / what to change.

**Backlog management:** Maintain a running backlog of tasks scored by ICE or business priority. Backlog grooming happens at the end of each sprint — add new ideas, re-score, remove stale items.

**Small team adaptation:** For teams of 1-3, daily standups can be async (written). Sprint length can flex to 1 week for high-velocity channels (paid) and 4 weeks for content-heavy sprints.

---

### 29. Objective-and-Task Budgeting

**What it is:** Builds the budget by defining the objective, then costing the tasks required to achieve it — bottom-up, evidence-based.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Action

**Step-by-step:**
1. State the specific objective (e.g., "Generate 500 MQLs in Q3")
2. Define required tasks (e.g., 10 blog posts, 4 paid campaigns, 2 webinars, 1 lead magnet)
3. Cost each task using market rates or internal capacity
4. Sum = budget request. Justify with the objective.

**Why superior to % of revenue:** % of revenue is backwards — it sizes the budget to past performance, not future goals. Objective-and-task links spend directly to outcomes.

**Common cost benchmarks (indicative, 2024-2025):**
| Item | Indicative Range |
|---|---|
| Blog post (freelance, 1,000-1,500 words) | £200–£600 |
| SEO platform (e.g., Semrush, Ahrefs) | £100–£400/month |
| Paid media management fee | 10–15% of ad spend |
| Email platform (mid-tier) | £50–£300/month |
| Video production (basic brand) | £1,500–£8,000 |
| Marketing automation platform | £200–£1,500/month |

### 30. Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

**What it is:** An organisational change framework — Create urgency, Build coalition, Form vision, Enlist volunteers, Remove barriers, Generate wins, Sustain acceleration, Institute change.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Action

**When to invoke:** Only when there is explicit organisational resistance to adopting the marketing plan — e.g., leadership misalignment, team culture conflict, or structural barriers to execution. Do not apply to standard campaign planning.

**Key steps most relevant to marketing change:** Step 1 (urgency from data), Step 2 (executive sponsor), Step 3 (clear marketing vision tied to business goal), Step 5 (remove approval blockers that slow campaign execution).

### 31. Attribution Models Comparison

**What it is:** Attribution models assign conversion credit to touchpoints in the customer journey. Different models produce different insights and incentivise different channel investments.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Control

| Model | Logic | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **First-touch** | 100% credit to first interaction | Long sales cycles, awareness investment |
| **Last-touch** | 100% credit to last interaction | Short cycles, direct response |
| **Linear** | Equal credit to all touches | Balanced multi-channel view |
| **Time-decay** | More credit to recent touches | Short-cycle, sales-led |
| **Position-based (U-shaped)** | 40% first, 40% last, 20% split middle | B2B, where first touch and close matter most |
| **W-shaped** | 30% first, 30% lead creation, 30% close, 10% other | Complex B2B with defined pipeline stages |
| **Data-driven** | ML assigns credit based on actual path data | High data volume, mature analytics |

**Selection guide:** Use last-touch for early-stage businesses with limited data. Move to position-based once multi-touch data is available. Consider data-driven only with 10k+ monthly conversions and robust analytics infrastructure.

**Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM):** For budgets above ~£500k/year, MMM provides econometric analysis of channel contribution independent of cookies/click tracking. Mention as future-state capability.

### 32. North Star Metric

**What it is:** A single metric that best captures the core value delivered to customers — if this metric grows, revenue and retention will follow.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Control

**Examples by business model:**
| Model | North Star Metric |
|---|---|
| SaaS / subscription | Weekly Active Users, or feature adoption rate |
| E-commerce | Monthly purchasing customers |
| Marketplace | Transactions per month (both sides active) |
| Media / content | Monthly engaged readers / watch time |
| Professional services | Clients achieving measurable outcome |

**How to select:** Ask "If this number grows consistently, does it guarantee the business is healthier?" If yes, it is a North Star candidate. Must be: (1) measurable, (2) actionable, (3) not a vanity metric, (4) not revenue itself — revenue is the outcome, not the cause.

**North Star ≠ revenue, ≠ page views, ≠ follower count.** These are either outcomes or vanity metrics that can grow while the business declines.

### 33. Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

**What it is:** Lagging indicators measure what already happened (revenue, CAC, churn). Leading indicators predict what is coming and allow course-correction before results deteriorate.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Control

**Paired examples by channel:**
| Channel | Leading Indicator | Lagging Indicator |
|---|---|---|
| SEO | Keyword ranking movement, indexed pages | Organic revenue, organic CAC |
| Email | Open rate, click rate, list growth | Revenue from email, churn from email |
| Paid | CTR, Quality Score, CPC trends | ROAS, paid CAC |
| Content | Time on page, social shares, return visitors | Lead volume from content, SEO traffic |
| CRO | Test velocity, variation performance | Conversion rate, revenue per visitor |

**Dashboard design rule:** Every dashboard should have at least one leading indicator per channel alongside the lagging metric. Lagging-only dashboards report the past; leading indicators drive decisions.

### 34. PDCA Cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act)

**What it is:** A four-stage iterative improvement loop — Plan (set hypothesis and goal), Do (execute), Check (measure results), Act (standardise if successful, or revise and repeat).

**SOSTAC Phase:** Control

**Marketing cadences:**
- **Weekly PDCA:** Paid campaign optimisation (bid adjustments, ad copy, audience)
- **Monthly PDCA:** Content performance review, email programme, SEO progress
- **Quarterly PDCA:** Full channel mix review, OKR scoring, strategy adjustment

**PDCA log template:**
| Date | Plan (hypothesis) | Do (what ran) | Check (result vs. goal) | Act (decision) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | |

**Usage note:** PDCA is the operating rhythm underneath the SOSTAC plan. Implement as a recurring team ritual, not a one-off exercise.

### 35. Balanced Scorecard for Marketing

**What it is:** Maps marketing performance across four perspectives — Financial, Customer, Internal Process, and Learning & Growth — ensuring short-term and long-term health are both tracked.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Control

**Marketing KPI mapping:**
| Perspective | Focus | Example KPIs |
|---|---|---|
| **Financial** | Revenue impact | MQL cost, CAC, ROAS, marketing-sourced revenue % |
| **Customer** | Experience and loyalty | NPS, CSAT, repeat purchase rate, CLV |
| **Internal Process** | Operational efficiency | Time-to-publish, campaign launch cycle time, test velocity |
| **Learning & Growth** | Capability building | Team skill index, tool adoption rate, experiment volume |

**Why use it:** Balanced Scorecard prevents over-optimisation on financial KPIs at the expense of customer experience and team capability — the two factors most likely to determine performance 12–24 months forward.

### 36. OKR Review Cadence

**What it is:** A structured rhythm for reviewing OKR progress — weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annually — to maintain alignment and enable timely course-correction.

**SOSTAC Phase:** Control

**Review rhythm:**

| Cadence | Format | Key Question | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Weekly** | Team standup or async update | Confidence score (0–100%): Is this OKR still on track? | Updated confidence % per KR |
| **Monthly** | 30-min review meeting | Are Key Results moving? What is blocking progress? | Action list, any KR amendments |
| **Quarterly** | Retrospective session | Score each KR (0.0–1.0). What did we learn? What resets? | Final scores, new OKR draft |
| **Annual** | Strategy reset | Did the Objective still matter? What changes at business level? | New annual OKRs |

**The 0.7 scoring convention:**
- **1.0:** Hit or exceeded — target was probably too easy
- **0.7:** Stretch achieved — ideal outcome
- **0.4–0.6:** Partial progress — investigate root cause
- **Below 0.4:** Missed — was this realistic? Was it prioritised? Were there blockers?

**Confidence score guidance:** A team consistently at 90–100% confidence week-over-week with a final score below 0.5 indicates either overconfidence in reporting or execution failure in the final weeks. Investigate early when weekly confidence drops below 50%.
