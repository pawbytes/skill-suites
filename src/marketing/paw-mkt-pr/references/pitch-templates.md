## 1. Cold Pitch Email Template

The universal structure that underlies all pitch types. Every pitch should contain
these five elements regardless of the specific format.

### Structure

```
SUBJECT LINE: [6-10 words. News hook, not company name. Under 36 characters ideal.]

Hi [First Name],

[PERSONALIZED OPENING -- 1-2 sentences referencing their recent work]
I read your piece on [specific article title/topic] in [Publication] -- [specific,
genuine observation about the piece, not generic flattery].

[THE STORY -- 2-3 sentences with the core news hook]
[What is the news? Why does it matter? Why now?]

[KEY DETAILS -- 2-4 bullet points with specifics]
- [Data point, finding, or fact #1]
- [Data point, finding, or fact #2]
- [Data point, finding, or fact #3]

[THE OFFER -- 1-2 sentences]
I can provide [exclusive data / an interview with our CEO / pre-release access /
expert commentary / high-res visuals]. Happy to share more details or jump on a
quick call.

Best,
[Name]
[Title], [Company]
[Email] | [Phone]
```

### Subject Line Formulas

| Formula | Example |
|---|---|
| New data: [surprising finding] | New data: 67% of remote workers skip lunch |
| Exclusive: [newsworthy claim] | Exclusive: Series B closes at $45M valuation |
| Expert source: [trending topic] | Expert source: AI hiring bias debate |
| Trend: [pattern] + data available | Trend: Gen Z quitting jobs faster -- data inside |
| [Brand] launches [product solving X] | Acme launches AI tool cutting hiring time 60% |
| [Number] + [surprising finding] | 3 in 4 CFOs expect recession by Q3 |

### Key Rules

- Keep total pitch under 200 words (not counting signature).
- No attachments on first contact. Link to assets or embed in body.
- Send from a personal email address, not a generic press@ inbox.
- Write in plain text or minimal HTML. Heavy formatting triggers spam filters.
- Include a clear, single ask. Do not offer five different story angles in one email.

## 2. Follow-Up Sequence

Never send "just checking in" follow-ups. Every touchpoint must add new value.

### Day 0: Initial Pitch

Send the full pitch per the templates in Section 3. This is the only email that
contains the complete story.

### Day 3: Follow-Up 1 -- New Angle

```
SUBJECT: Re: [Original subject line]

Hi [First Name],

Quick follow-up on [topic]. Since I reached out, [new development]:

- [New data point, angle, or timely hook that was not in the original pitch]

[One sentence restating the core offer]. Let me know if this is a fit.

Best,
[Name]
```

Purpose: Add a genuinely new piece of information -- a fresh data point, a new angle
connected to breaking news, an additional expert who is now available, or updated
numbers.

### Day 7: Follow-Up 2 -- Timely Hook

```
SUBJECT: Re: [Original subject line]

Hi [First Name],

Saw your recent piece on [their recent article]. [Brief genuine comment.]

Our [data/expert/story] ties directly into this -- [one sentence connecting your
pitch to their recent coverage].

Happy to share [specific asset] if helpful for a follow-up piece. No worries if
the timing doesn't work.

Best,
[Name]
```

Purpose: Connect your pitch to something the journalist just published or a current
news event. This demonstrates you are paying attention and the story is timely.

### Day 14: Move On

Do not send a third follow-up on the same pitch. If there has been no response after
two follow-ups:

- Mark the journalist as "pitched, no response" in your tracker.
- Wait at least 4-6 weeks before pitching the same journalist on a different story.
- Consider whether the pitch simply was not a fit for their beat.
- Keep engaging with their work on social media to maintain visibility.

### Follow-Up Rules

- Always reply to the original thread (same subject line with Re:).
- Space follow-ups 3-4 days apart. Shorter gaps feel pushy.
- Each follow-up should be shorter than the previous one.
- If they respond with "not right now," thank them and move on.
- If they respond with interest, reply within 1 hour with everything they need.
- Track all follow-ups in your outreach CRM (BuzzStream, Pitchbox, or spreadsheet).

## 3. Pitch Types

### 3.1 News Pitch (Product Launch / Company News)

```
SUBJECT: [Brand] launches [product] -- [key benefit in numbers]

Hi [First Name],

Your coverage of [related topic/competitor] in [Publication] caught my eye --
particularly [specific detail].

[Brand] just launched [Product], which [one sentence: what it does and why it
matters to the journalist's audience].

Key details:
- [Differentiator #1 with specific metric]
- [Differentiator #2 with social proof or data]
- [Availability, pricing, or access details]

I can offer [demo access / founder interview / press kit with images and specs].
Would a [5-minute call / press kit] be useful?

Best,
[Name]
```

### 3.2 Expert Commentary Pitch

```
SUBJECT: Expert source: [trending topic]

Hi [First Name],

Your recent piece on [specific article] raised important questions about [topic].

[Expert Name], [Title] at [Company], has [specific credential: years of experience,
relevant background, published research]. [He/She/They] can provide [expert
commentary / data-backed perspective / contrarian insight] on:

- "[One quotable sentence that demonstrates the quality of commentary]"
- [Second specific angle they can speak to]

[Expert Name] is available for a [phone call / written quote / video interview]
on deadline. I can also send a full written response within [timeframe].

Best,
[Name]
```

### 3.3 Data Study Pitch

```
SUBJECT: New data: [most surprising finding with number]

Hi [First Name],

Given your coverage of [beat/topic], I thought you'd find this relevant.

We [surveyed X people / analyzed X data points / compiled X public records] and
found [headline finding].

Top findings:
- [Finding #1 with specific percentage or number]
- [Finding #2 with specific percentage or number]
- [Finding #3 with specific percentage or number]

Full methodology: [sample size, margin of error, timeframe]. I can provide the
complete data set, visualizations, and expert commentary from [Spokesperson Name,
Title].

[Optional: Would you like exclusive access before the public release on [date]?]

Best,
[Name]
```

### 3.4 Trend Piece Pitch

```
SUBJECT: Trend: [pattern] -- data and expert available

Hi [First Name],

I've been following your reporting on [beat] and noticed you've covered [related
trend] recently.

We're seeing [describe the trend in one sentence], supported by:
- [Data point #1 -- from your data or third-party research]
- [Data point #2]
- [Anecdotal evidence or customer insight]

[Expert Name], [Title] at [Company], has been [tracking this / working on solutions
for this / researching this] for [timeframe] and can provide [data-backed
commentary / customer case studies / predictions].

Would this fit a story you're working on? Happy to connect you directly.

Best,
[Name]
```

### 3.5 Exclusive Pitch

```
SUBJECT: Exclusive: [newsworthy claim -- specific and compelling]

Hi [First Name],

I'm reaching out with an exclusive for [Publication] because [specific reason this
fits their audience/beat].

[One paragraph: the news, why it matters, key numbers. This should be strong enough
to convince the journalist to take the exclusive.]

Key details:
- [Most important fact]
- [Second most important fact]
- [Scale / impact / what makes this significant]

We're offering this exclusively to [Publication] with a [date] embargo. If
[Journalist First Name] is interested, I can provide [full press kit / data /
interview access / advance product access] immediately.

Please let me know by [deadline -- typically 48-72 hours] so we can finalize
our distribution plan.

Best,
[Name]
```

### 3.6 Embargo Pitch

```
SUBJECT: Embargoed: [brief description of news] -- [embargo date]

Hi [First Name],

We have news we'd like to share under embargo ahead of our [date] announcement.
Before I send details, I want to confirm you're comfortable with an embargo
lifting on [date, time, timezone].

The short version: [1-2 sentences summarizing the news at a high level without
revealing confidential details].

If you're interested and can agree to the embargo terms, I'll send the full
press kit with [data / images / interview access].

Best,
[Name]
```

Important: Never send embargoed material until the journalist explicitly agrees
to the embargo terms. Get confirmation in writing (email reply is sufficient).

## 4. Press Release Template

```markdown
# [HEADLINE: Active voice, newsworthy, under 80 characters]
## [Subheadline: Expands with a key detail or context]

**[CITY, State] -- [YYYY-MM-DD]** --

[Lead paragraph: Who, what, when, where, why in 2-3 sentences. The single most
important information. If someone reads only this paragraph, they should
understand the news.]

[Second paragraph: Supporting detail, context, significance. Why does this matter
to the reader? What is the broader impact?]

"[Quote from company spokesperson -- adds opinion, vision, or personality. Never
restates facts already in the release.]" -- [Full Name], [Title], [Company].

[Body paragraph 1: Additional details, data, background. Specifics and numbers.]

[Body paragraph 2: Customer/partner/analyst context. Social proof or third-party
validation.]

[Optional second quote from customer, partner, or industry analyst.]

[CTA paragraph: Where to learn more, sign up, or get involved. Include URL.]

### About [Company Name]
[Boilerplate: 2-3 sentences. What the company does, when founded, key metrics
(customers, team size, funding), mission. Update quarterly.]

### Media Contact
[Name] | [Email] | [Phone]
[Response time commitment, e.g., "Responses within 2 hours during business hours"]
```

### Press Release Guidelines

- **400-600 words** total. Shorter is better.
- **Inverted pyramid**: Most important information first. Each paragraph should stand
  alone if the rest were cut.
- **Newsworthiness test**: Funding rounds, major partnerships, original data, significant
  milestones qualify. Internal promotions and minor features do not.
- **Quotes add insight**: Quotes should provide opinion, vision, or personality that
  a journalist cannot write themselves. Never restate facts.
- **Data and specifics**: Concrete numbers outperform vague superlatives.
- **Modern enhancements**: Embed or link to high-res images, video, infographics.
  Visual assets increase pickup 2-3x. Include 2-3 social-ready pull quotes.
- **SEO optimization**: Include target keywords naturally in the headline and first
  paragraph. Structure for AI parsability with clear entity names and data points.

## 5. Media Kit Checklist

Every brand should maintain an up-to-date media kit. Contents:

### Required

- [ ] **Company overview**: 2-3 paragraphs (what the company does, founding story,
  mission, key differentiator)
- [ ] **Key facts**: Founded, HQ location, team size, customer count, 3-5 key
  milestones
- [ ] **Leadership bios**: 2-3 sentences each, written in third person, with
  credentials and areas of expertise
- [ ] **Leadership headshots**: Professional, high-resolution (300 DPI minimum),
  downloadable in multiple sizes
- [ ] **Brand assets**: Logo pack (PNG + SVG, color + monochrome + reversed),
  brand colors (hex + RGB), approved fonts, usage guidelines
- [ ] **Media contact**: Name, email, phone, response time commitment

### Recommended

- [ ] **Recent press coverage**: Table with date, outlet, headline, link (5-10 most
  notable pieces)
- [ ] **Awards and recognition**: List with year and granting organization
- [ ] **Product screenshots / images**: High-resolution, with captions
- [ ] **Fact sheet / one-pager**: PDF with key company information at a glance
- [ ] **Spokesperson availability**: Who can speak on which topics, timezone,
  response time

### Press Page

Host at `/press` or `/newsroom` on the company website:

- All media kit contents above, downloadable
- Press releases in reverse chronological order
- "In the News" section with recent coverage links
- Update monthly. An outdated press page signals an inactive brand.

## 6. Spokesperson Preparation

### 6.1 The Message Triangle

For every media opportunity, prepare three layers:

```
            [CORE MESSAGE]
         (1 sentence -- the single
        most important takeaway)
           /              \
    [SUPPORTING          [SUPPORTING
     MESSAGE 1]           MESSAGE 2]
    (with proof)          (with proof)
         |                     |
    [SUPPORTING MESSAGE 3]
         (with proof)
```

- **Core message**: One sentence. If the journalist remembers only one thing, this is it.
- **Supporting messages**: Maximum 3. Each backed by a proof point (data, case study,
  third-party validation, or specific example).
- **Proof points**: Statistics, customer stories, analyst quotes, awards, third-party
  data. Never make a claim without a proof point.

### 6.2 Bridging Phrases

When the conversation drifts off-message, use bridging phrases to redirect:

| Situation | Bridging Phrase |
|---|---|
| Off-topic question | "That's an important area. What's most relevant to what we're seeing is..." |
| Hostile framing | "I'd characterize it differently. What's actually happening is..." |
| Speculative question | "I can't speculate on that, but what I can tell you is..." |
| Competitor comparison | "I can't speak for them, but what our customers value is..." |
| Confidential topic | "We're not ready to discuss that yet, but here's what we can share..." |
| Negative premise | "Let me put that in context..." |
| Rumor | "The facts are..." |

### 6.3 Q&A Preparation Document

For every media interview, prepare:

```markdown
## Interview Prep: [Topic] -- [Date]

### Journalist
- Name: [Full name]
- Outlet: [Publication]
- Beat: [Their focus area]
- Recent articles: [Links to 3-5 recent pieces]
- Style notes: [Conversational? Adversarial? Deep-dive? Quick-hit?]

### Our Objectives
1. [Primary message we want to convey]
2. [Secondary message]
3. [Tertiary message]

### Anticipated Questions & Approved Responses

**Q: [Most likely question]**
A: [Approved response -- written in conversational language, under 30 seconds spoken]

**Q: [Second likely question]**
A: [Approved response]

**Q: [Potentially difficult question]**
A: [Approved response with bridging phrase]

**Q: [Potentially difficult question #2]**
A: [Approved response]

### Off-Limits Topics
- [Topic 1]: Redirect with "[bridging phrase] + [approved message]"
- [Topic 2]: Redirect with "[bridging phrase] + [approved message]"

### Key Data Points
- [Stat 1 -- memorize this]
- [Stat 2]
- [Stat 3]
```

### 6.4 Interview Best Practices

**Before**:
- Research the journalist and outlet. Read their last 5 articles.
- Practice key messages aloud, each in under 30 seconds.
- Prepare 2-3 anecdotes that illustrate key points.
- Test audio/video setup for virtual interviews.

**During**:
- Lead with your strongest message within the first 60 seconds.
- Speak in quotable sentences. Short, declarative, specific.
- Use bridging phrases to redirect, never say "no comment."
- Provide specific examples and data rather than generalities.
- Pause before answering difficult questions. Silence is acceptable.
- If you do not know the answer, say so and offer to follow up.

**After**:
- Send a thank-you email within 2 hours.
- Provide any promised follow-up materials immediately.
- Share the published piece and tag the journalist.

## 7. HARO / Source Request Response Template

```
SUBJECT: Re: [Exact query subject line from the platform]

Hi [Journalist First Name],

[CREDENTIALS -- 1-2 sentences]
I'm [Full Name], [Title] at [Company]. I've [specific experience relevant to this
query: years in the field, published research, direct experience, client work,
certifications].

[DIRECT ANSWER -- 2-4 quotable sentences]
[Answer the journalist's exact question directly. Write in a voice that can be
copied verbatim into an article. Be specific, not generic. Include a data point
or concrete example if possible.]

[UNIQUE ANGLE -- 1-2 sentences]
[Add something the journalist likely has not heard from other respondents: a
contrarian perspective, a proprietary data point, a specific first-hand example,
or a forward-looking prediction.]

[ASSETS]
- Headshot: [Link to high-resolution headshot]
- Bio: [2-3 sentence professional bio]
- Website: [URL]

Happy to expand on any of this or provide additional context.

Best,
[Full Name]
[Title], [Company]
[Email] | [Phone]
```

### HARO Response Rules

- Respond within 1-2 hours. Speed is the single biggest success factor.
- Keep the entire response under 200 words (excluding bio and assets).
- Answer the specific question asked. Do not pitch your product.
- Write in quotable sentences. Make it easy to copy into an article.
- Always include headshot and bio. Journalists skip responses without them.
- Do not respond to queries outside your genuine expertise.
- Track every response: date, platform, query topic, journalist, outcome.

## 8. Podcast Pitch Template

```
SUBJECT: Guest pitch: [Specific topic] for [Podcast Name]

Hi [Host First Name],

I just listened to your episode with [Recent Guest Name] on [Topic] -- [specific,
genuine observation about the episode, not generic flattery. Reference a particular
point, question, or moment that resonated].

I'd love to come on [Podcast Name] to discuss [specific topic]. Here's what I
can bring to the conversation:

1. [Talking point #1 -- specific, not generic]
2. [Talking point #2 -- with data or unique angle]
3. [Talking point #3 -- something provocative or counterintuitive]

About me: [2-3 sentences. Title, company, specific credentials relevant to the
proposed topic. Mention audience size if relevant.]

Previous appearances: [Link to 1-2 podcast episodes or speaking videos]

I'm happy to promote the episode to our [audience size] community across
[channels].

Best,
[Name]
[Title], [Company]
[Website] | [LinkedIn]
```

### Podcast Pitch Rules

- Reference a specific recent episode, not just the podcast in general.
- Propose 3 specific talking points, not "I'd love to share my story."
- Include proof of speaking ability (links to previous episodes or videos).
- Offer to promote the episode to your audience.
- Target podcasts your audience listens to, not just the biggest shows.
- Check the guest backlog -- many podcasts book 2-4 months in advance.

## 9. Speaking Pitch Template

```
SUBJECT: Speaker proposal: [Talk Title] for [Event Name]

Hi [Organizer First Name],

I'm writing to propose a session for [Event Name / Conference] on [Date/Year]:

**Talk title**: [Specific, compelling title -- not generic]

**Description**: [2-3 sentences. What the audience will learn and why it matters
now. Focus on takeaways, not the speaker's background.]

**Key takeaways for attendees**:
1. [Specific, actionable takeaway #1]
2. [Specific, actionable takeaway #2]
3. [Specific, actionable takeaway #3]

**Format**: [Keynote / Panel / Workshop / Fireside chat]. [Duration] minutes.
Happy to adapt to what works best for the program.

**About me**: [3-4 sentences. Title, credentials, and why this person is qualified
to deliver this talk. Include relevant metrics, publications, or experience.]

**Previous speaking**: [List 2-3 recent events. Link to video if available.]

I can customize this talk to align with [Event Name]'s theme of [theme if known].
Happy to discuss further.

Best,
[Name]
[Title], [Company]
[Website] | [LinkedIn]
```

### Speaking Pitch Tips

- Apply 6-12 months in advance for major conferences.
- Propose a specific talk, not "I'd love to speak at your event."
- Focus on audience value, not self-promotion.
- Include video evidence of speaking ability when available.
- Offer to customize for the event's theme and audience.
- Follow up once after 2 weeks if no response.

## 10. Crisis Statement Templates

### 10.1 Initial Holding Statement (Within 1-2 Hours)

Use when the crisis has been detected but facts are still being gathered.

```
[Company Name] Statement Regarding [Brief Description of Situation]

We are aware of [brief, factual description of the situation]. We take this matter
seriously and are actively investigating.

[If people are affected]: Our immediate priority is [the safety / support / well-being]
of [those affected].

We are working to understand the full scope of this situation and will provide an
update by [specific time -- commit to a deadline, typically within 4-8 hours].

For questions, please contact [Name] at [Email] / [Phone].
```

Key rules for holding statements:
- Acknowledge the situation. Do not ignore or minimize.
- Express seriousness without admitting fault (legal may need to review).
- Commit to a specific time for the next update. Meet that deadline.
- Do not speculate, assign blame, or provide details you cannot confirm.

### 10.2 Full Response Statement (When Facts Are Clear)

```
[Company Name] Statement on [Situation]

On [date], [factual description of what happened -- be specific and honest].

[IMPACT]: [Who was affected and how. Be specific about scope.]

[ACCOUNTABILITY]: [Take responsibility where appropriate. "We fell short of our
standards" or "This was caused by [specific cause]." Avoid passive voice and
corporate euphemisms.]

[ACTION TAKEN]: [What has been done so far to address the immediate issue.]

[PREVENTION]: [Specific steps being taken to prevent recurrence. Be concrete:
"We have implemented X" not "We are reviewing our processes."]

[SUPPORT]: [What is being offered to those affected. Specific remedies, not
vague promises.]

We will provide further updates as [additional information becomes available /
our review is completed / specific milestone]. Our next update will be on [date].

[Spokesperson Name], [Title]
[Company Name]
```

### 10.3 Update Statement (During Ongoing Crisis)

```
Update: [Company Name] Statement on [Situation] -- [Date, Time]

This is an update to our statement issued on [previous date].

[NEW INFORMATION]: [What has been learned since the last update. Facts only.]

[PROGRESS]: [What actions have been completed since the last update.]

[NEXT STEPS]: [What is being done next, with timeline.]

[ONGOING SUPPORT]: [Remind affected parties of available resources/remedies.]

Our next update will be on [date/time] or sooner if significant new information
becomes available.

[Spokesperson Name], [Title]
[Company Name]
```

### 10.4 Resolution Statement (Crisis Resolved)

```
[Company Name] Final Update on [Situation]

Following our investigation into [brief description], we want to share our
findings and the steps we've taken.

[ROOT CAUSE]: [Clear, honest explanation of what caused the situation.]

[FULL SCOPE]: [Complete picture of impact -- who was affected, extent of damage.]

[REMEDIATION]: [Everything that has been done to address the situation and support
those affected.]

[SYSTEMIC CHANGES]: [Specific, permanent changes implemented to prevent recurrence.
Be concrete and verifiable.]

[COMMITMENT]: [Forward-looking statement about standards and accountability.]

We recognize that trust is earned through action, not words. We are committed to
[specific ongoing measures] and will [reporting mechanism for accountability,
e.g., publish a follow-up report in 90 days].

To those affected: [Direct, empathetic message. Thank them for patience. Reiterate
support resources.]

[CEO/Spokesperson Name], [Title]
[Company Name]
```

### Crisis Communication Principles

1. **Speed over perfection**: A holding statement within 1-2 hours beats a perfect
   statement 12 hours later. Fill the information vacuum before others do.
2. **Specificity over vagueness**: "We are reviewing our processes" means nothing.
   "We have hired an independent auditor and will publish results in 30 days" builds trust.
3. **Empathy before facts**: Lead with care for those affected. Facts and details come
   second.
4. **One spokesperson**: All public statements come from one designated person. Mixed
   messages amplify crisis.
5. **Commit to timelines**: Promise the next update by a specific time and meet that
   deadline, even if the update is "we are still investigating."
6. **Assume public**: Every internal message, every DM, every email may become public.
   Write accordingly.
7. **No "no comment"**: Always provide something. "We are actively investigating and
   will share details by [time]" is always better than silence.
8. **Post-crisis follow-through**: Announce changes and follow through visibly. Broken
   promises after a crisis are worse than the crisis itself.

## Quick Reference: Pitch Checklist

Before sending any pitch, verify:

- [ ] Subject line is under 10 words and leads with the news hook
- [ ] Opening sentence references the journalist's recent work
- [ ] The story is clear in the first 2 sentences
- [ ] Specific data or facts are included (not just claims)
- [ ] There is a clear, single offer (exclusive, interview, data, demo)
- [ ] Total pitch is under 200 words
- [ ] No attachments (links to assets instead)
- [ ] Sent from a personal email, not a generic inbox
- [ ] Sent Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10 AM in journalist's timezone
- [ ] Journalist's beat matches the story
- [ ] Follow-up plan is scheduled (Day 3, Day 7)
