# PR & Crisis Communication Pipeline Workflow

This reference defines the dual-track workflow for proactive PR and crisis communication, diagnostic questions, benchmark data, deliverables, and the ethical guidelines that govern all PR work.

---

## Core Principle: Human Review is Mandatory

**Never send an unedited AI draft to a journalist.** Journalists receive hundreds of pitches daily. An AI-generated pitch that reads like AI-generated copy will be deleted immediately and may damage the sender's professional reputation permanently. Every press release, pitch email, and public statement must be reviewed, edited, and approved by a human before it leaves the organization.

This principle applies across both tracks:
- **Proactive PR**: Pitches and press releases must reflect genuine human judgment, voice, and editorial quality.
- **Crisis Communication**: Statements issued under pressure must be factually verified, legally reviewed, and authorized by a designated spokesperson.

---

## Diagnostic Questions

Ask these before producing any PR recommendation. PR without context is noise.

### For Proactive PR (Track A)
1. **Objective**: What is the PR goal? (Brand awareness, product launch, thought leadership, link building, investor relations, recruitment?)
2. **News angle**: What makes this newsworthy right now? Why would a journalist's audience care?
3. **Target media**: What publications and journalists are the priority? (Tier 1/2/3, trade vs. mainstream, local vs. national?)
4. **Timing**: Is there a hard deadline (launch date, embargo)? Are there competing news events to avoid?
5. **Assets**: What supporting materials exist? (Data, images, quotes, demos, case studies?)
6. **History**: What PR has been done before? What earned coverage? What got ignored?
7. **Spokesperson**: Who will speak to media? Are they media-trained?

### For Crisis Communication (Track B)
1. **Nature**: What happened? What are the confirmed facts? What is still unknown?
2. **Severity**: How many stakeholders are affected? Is there legal, financial, or physical risk?
3. **Velocity**: How fast is this spreading? (Social media, press inquiries, internal reports?)
4. **Stakeholders**: Who needs to be informed? (Customers, employees, investors, regulators, media, public?)
5. **Response authority**: Who is authorized to issue public statements? Who is the legal contact?
6. **Channels**: Where are people talking about this? (Twitter/X, Reddit, news sites, internal channels?)
7. **Precedent**: Has anything similar happened before? What was the response and outcome?

If the user cannot answer questions 1-2 for Track A, recommend a news angle development workshop before pitching. Pitching without a strong angle wastes journalist relationships.

## Track A: Proactive PR (Press Release / Media Campaign)

### Phase A1: News Angle Development

#### Purpose
Identify the newsworthy angle, target audience, and timing that gives the story the best chance of earning coverage.

#### Entry Conditions
- Brand context and SOSTAC plan available (or brand intake completed)
- Product, event, or development to announce
- Basic understanding of target media landscape

#### Key Activities
1. **Apply the newsworthiness test** -- a story must pass at least 2 of these 5 criteria:

   | Criterion | Question | Example |
   |---|---|---|
   | Timeliness | Is this happening now or tied to a current event? | Product launch, trend commentary |
   | Impact | Does this affect a significant number of people? | Industry-wide pricing change |
   | Novelty | Is this genuinely new or first-of-its-kind? | First AI feature in the category |
   | Conflict/Tension | Does this challenge conventional wisdom? | Data contradicting popular belief |
   | Human interest | Is there a compelling personal story? | Founder origin story, customer transformation |

2. **Develop the angle** (not the announcement -- the story):
   - The announcement: "We launched a new feature" (not newsworthy)
   - The angle: "Our data shows 73% of marketers waste budget on X -- here's what we built to fix it" (newsworthy)
   - Lead with the insight, data, or trend -- not the product

3. **Identify the media tier targets**:

   | Tier | Definition | Examples | Approach |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | Tier 1 | Major national / global outlets | TechCrunch, Forbes, WSJ, NYT | Exclusive angle, data, embargo |
   | Tier 2 | Industry-leading trade publications | SaaStr, MarketingProfs, Product Hunt | Expert commentary, case studies |
   | Tier 3 | Niche blogs, podcasts, newsletters | Industry newsletters, podcast interviews | Broader pitch, guest contributions |

4. **Timing assessment**:
   - Avoid: major holidays, election weeks, industry mega-events (unless your news ties to them)
   - Leverage: seasonal trends, awareness months, industry events, news cycles you can tie into
   - Embargo strategy: offer exclusives to Tier 1 with a 24-48 hour embargo

#### Deliverables
- News angle brief: angle, newsworthiness criteria met, timing, tier targets
- Key messages (3 maximum): the core points every piece of coverage should include

#### Exit Conditions
- Angle passes newsworthiness test (minimum 2 of 5 criteria)
- Angle articulated in one compelling sentence
- Timing confirmed and no conflicting events identified

---

### Phase A2: Media List Building

#### Purpose
Build a targeted, researched media list of journalists who cover this beat, write for the target audience, and are likely to be interested in this specific angle.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase A1 complete: angle and tier targets defined
- Research tools available (Muck Rack, Cision, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, or manual research)

#### Key Activities
1. **Journalist research** -- quality over quantity:
   - Target 30-50 journalists maximum (not 500 generic names)
   - For each journalist, document: name, outlet, beat, recent relevant articles (3 minimum), contact info, social handles
   - Read their last 5-10 articles to understand what they cover, how they frame stories, and what they care about

2. **Beat matching** -- match your angle to their beat:
   - A product launch pitch to a reporter who covers M&A = deleted
   - A data story about marketing trends to a reporter who covers marketing technology = relevant
   - Search for journalists who have covered: your competitors, your category, the trend your angle ties to

3. **Relationship mapping**:
   - Have we interacted with this journalist before? (Previous coverage, event meetings, social engagement?)
   - Has anyone on the team been quoted by them?
   - Do we have a mutual connection who can make an introduction?
   - What is their preferred contact method? (Some prefer email, some DM, some are on specific platforms)

4. **Engagement before pitching** (2-4 weeks before the pitch):
   - Follow on social media
   - Share and comment on their articles with genuine, substantive reactions
   - Respond to their queries on HARO/Connectively/Qwoted
   - Build familiarity before making an ask

5. **Media list format**:

   | Journalist | Outlet | Beat | Recent Article | Contact | Notes |
   |---|---|---|---|---|---|
   | Name | Publication | Specific beat | Title + link | Email, Twitter | Prior relationship, preferences |

#### Deliverables
- Targeted media list (30-50 contacts with research notes)
- Relationship status for each journalist (cold, warm, existing)
- Pre-pitch engagement plan

#### Exit Conditions
- Media list complete with beat-matched journalists
- Research notes include recent articles and coverage patterns
- Pre-pitch engagement initiated (if timeline allows)

### Phase A3: Press Release / Pitch

#### Purpose
Draft the press release or pitch email that tells the story in a way that serves the journalist's audience first.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase A2 complete: media list built and research done
- Supporting assets ready (data, quotes, images, demo access)

#### Key Activities
1. **Press release structure** (if issuing a formal release):

   ```
   [HEADLINE] -- Clear, specific, newsworthy (not a marketing tagline)
   [SUBHEADLINE] -- Supporting context
   [DATELINE] -- City, Date
   
   [LEAD PARAGRAPH] -- Who, what, when, where, why in 2-3 sentences
   [CONTEXT PARAGRAPH] -- Why this matters now, industry context, data
   [QUOTE 1] -- Executive quote adding human perspective (not corporate speak)
   [DETAILS] -- Product/feature specifics, availability, pricing
   [QUOTE 2] -- Customer or partner quote (social proof)
   [BOILERPLATE] -- Company description (2-3 sentences)
   [MEDIA CONTACT] -- Name, email, phone
   ```

2. **Pitch email structure** (for personalized outreach):
   - **Subject line**: 6-10 words, specific, references their beat. No: "Exciting announcement from [Company]." Yes: "Data: 73% of marketers waste budget on X -- exclusive"
   - **Opening line**: Reference their recent work. Prove you have read their coverage.
   - **The hook**: 2-3 sentences on why this story matters to their audience
   - **The offer**: What you can provide (exclusive data, interview, demo, early access)
   - **The ask**: Specific and low-friction ("Would a 15-minute call with our CEO be useful for your piece on X?")
   - **Total length**: 150-200 words maximum. Journalists do not read long pitches.

3. **Pitch personalization levels**:

   | Level | Effort | Response Rate | When to Use |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | Deeply personalized | High (15-20 min/pitch) | 15-25% | Tier 1 targets, exclusive offers |
   | Semi-personalized | Medium (5-10 min/pitch) | 8-15% | Tier 2, referenced recent article |
   | Template-based | Low (1-2 min/pitch) | 2-5% | Tier 3, broad awareness |

4. **Quote writing guidelines**:
   - Quotes should sound like a real person speaking, not a press release
   - Weak: "We are excited to announce our innovative solution"
   - Strong: "We spent 18 months studying why marketing teams lose 40% of their budget to attribution gaps. This tool is the fix."
   - Every quote should add context, opinion, or insight -- not restate the announcement

5. **Pre-send checklist**:

   | Check | Status |
   |---|---|
   | Headline is newsworthy (not a marketing tagline) | |
   | Lead paragraph answers who/what/when/where/why | |
   | Pitch is under 200 words | |
   | Personalization references journalist's recent work | |
   | Subject line is specific and under 10 words | |
   | Supporting materials (data, images, links) attached or linked | |
   | Quotes sound human, not corporate | |
   | Contact information is correct | |
   | **Human review and approval received** | |

#### Deliverables
- Press release (if applicable): complete draft with headline, quotes, boilerplate
- Pitch email templates: Tier 1 (deeply personalized), Tier 2 (semi-personalized), Tier 3 (template)
- Supporting materials package: fact sheet, images, data, demo access

#### Exit Conditions
- All materials pass the pre-send checklist
- **Human review gate**: Every pitch and press release reviewed and approved by a human before sending
- Supporting assets packaged and accessible (hosted link, not large attachments)

### Phase A4: Outreach Execution

#### Purpose
Execute the personalized outreach campaign with strategic timing, follow-up cadence, and exclusive offer management.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase A3 complete: pitches approved and supporting materials ready
- Media list finalized with personalization notes

#### Key Activities
1. **Outreach sequence**:
   - **Day 0**: Send initial pitch (Tier 1 exclusives first, with 24-48 hour head start)
   - **Day 3-4**: Follow-up #1 (add new data point or angle, not "just checking in")
   - **Day 7-8**: Follow-up #2 (final follow-up, offer alternative angle or different story)
   - **Maximum 3 touches**. After that, move on. Persistent follow-up damages relationships.

2. **Follow-up principles**:
   - Every follow-up must add value (new data, timely hook, different angle)
   - Never: "Just wanted to bump this to the top of your inbox"
   - Instead: "Since I emailed, [new development] makes this more relevant because..."
   - Keep follow-ups shorter than the original pitch

3. **Exclusive management**:
   - Offer Tier 1 outlets first right of refusal with a defined window (24-48 hours)
   - If declined, move to next Tier 1 target
   - Once exclusive window expires, broaden to full list
   - Track who has exclusive, when it expires, and communicate clearly

4. **Outreach tracking**:

   | Journalist | Outlet | Sent Date | Follow-up 1 | Follow-up 2 | Response | Coverage |
   |---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
   | Name | Publication | Date | Date | Date | Status | Link |

5. **Response handling**:
   - Positive interest: respond within 2 hours with requested materials
   - Questions: answer promptly and thoroughly
   - Decline: thank them, ask if there is a better angle for future
   - No response after 3 touches: mark as unresponsive, do not follow up further on this story

#### Deliverables
- Outreach tracker: every contact, date, follow-up status, response
- Follow-up email variants (2 per tier)
- Exclusive offer tracking document

#### Exit Conditions
- All outreach sent according to tier strategy
- Follow-up cadence completed (maximum 3 touches)
- All responses handled promptly
- Exclusive windows tracked and honored

---

### Phase A5: Coverage Tracking

#### Purpose
Monitor and measure the earned coverage from the campaign, track backlinks, analyze sentiment, and calculate share of voice.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase A4 complete: outreach executed
- Monitoring tools in place

#### Key Activities
1. **Coverage monitoring**:
   - Set up Google Alerts for brand name, campaign keywords, spokesperson names
   - Use media monitoring tools: Muck Rack, Mention, Brand24, or Cision
   - Check daily for 2 weeks post-campaign, then weekly for 4 weeks
   - Save all coverage with: outlet, headline, link, date, journalist, tier, sentiment

2. **Backlink tracking** (PR as link building):
   - Every piece of coverage should include a backlink to your site
   - Track with Ahrefs, Moz, or Semrush: referring domain, DA/DR, anchor text
   - If coverage exists without a link, politely request one ("Would you be able to link to [URL] for reader context?")
   - Target: 30-50% of coverage includes a dofollow backlink

3. **Sentiment analysis**:
   - Positive: brand presented favorably, key messages included
   - Neutral: brand mentioned factually, no editorial opinion
   - Negative: critical coverage, issues highlighted
   - Track sentiment ratio across all coverage

4. **Share of voice** (SOV):
   - Compare your brand mentions to competitors in the same timeframe
   - Formula: (Your mentions / Total category mentions) x 100
   - Track SOV monthly and by outlet tier

5. **PR performance benchmarks**:

   | Metric | Excellent | Good | Needs Work |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | Pitch response rate | >20% | 10-20% | <10% |
   | Coverage rate (pitches to stories) | >15% | 5-15% | <5% |
   | Tier 1 placements per campaign | 2+ | 1 | 0 |
   | Backlinks earned per campaign | 10+ | 3-10 | <3 |
   | Average DA of linking domains | 50+ | 30-50 | <30 |
   | Key message inclusion rate | >80% | 50-80% | <50% |
   | Sentiment (positive + neutral) | >90% | 70-90% | <70% |

6. **Coverage amplification**:
   - Share coverage on social media with attribution
   - Add "As featured in..." to website with outlet logos
   - Include coverage in email newsletters
   - Use quotes from coverage in sales collateral
   - Route to paw-mkt-social for social amplification strategy

#### Deliverables
- Coverage report: all placements with outlet, date, link, tier, sentiment
- Backlink report: new referring domains, DA, anchor text
- SOV analysis: brand mentions vs. competitors
- Campaign ROI summary

#### Exit Conditions
- All coverage tracked and catalogued
- Backlinks monitored and link requests sent where coverage lacks links
- Campaign performance compared against benchmarks
- Learnings documented for next campaign

## Track B: Crisis Communication (Time-Critical)

### Severity Classification

Before entering Track B, classify the crisis severity. This determines response speed and escalation level.

| Level | Definition | Examples | Response Window |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 1: Low** | Internal issue, limited external awareness | Minor bug, small customer complaint, internal process failure | 24-48 hours |
| **Level 2: Medium** | Growing external awareness, moderate stakeholder impact | Negative review trend, minor data issue, product outage (<4 hours) | 4-8 hours |
| **Level 3: High** | Significant external attention, major stakeholder impact | Data breach, major outage, executive misconduct, safety issue | 1-2 hours |
| **Level 4: Critical** | National/global media attention, existential risk | Major data breach, legal action, regulatory investigation, physical harm | Immediately (within 1 hour) |

---

### Phase B1: Assessment (0-1 Hour)

#### Purpose
Rapidly assess the crisis: what happened, how severe it is, who is affected, and what the immediate risks are. Speed and accuracy matter equally.

#### Entry Conditions
- Crisis event identified or reported
- Initial information available (even if incomplete)

#### Key Activities
1. **Fact gathering** (first 30 minutes):
   - What happened? (Confirmed facts only -- separate facts from rumors)
   - When did it happen? When was it discovered?
   - Who is affected? (Customers, employees, partners, public?)
   - What is the current scope? (Number affected, geographic scope, severity)
   - Is the situation ongoing or contained?
   - What do we NOT know yet?

2. **Severity classification** (see table above):
   - Classify based on confirmed facts, not worst-case assumptions
   - Reclassify as new information emerges
   - When in doubt, classify one level higher

3. **Stakeholder identification**:

   | Stakeholder | Priority | Communication Channel | Timing |
   |---|---|---|---|
   | Affected customers | Highest | Direct email, in-app notification | Immediate |
   | All customers | High | Email, status page, social media | Within 2-4 hours |
   | Employees | High | Internal comms (Slack, email, all-hands) | Within 1 hour |
   | Investors / board | High | Direct call or email | Within 2 hours |
   | Media | Medium | Holding statement, press inquiry response | Within 1-2 hours |
   | Regulators | Medium-High | Formal notification per requirements | Per regulation |
   | Partners | Medium | Direct communication | Within 4 hours |
   | General public | Lower | Social media, website, press release | Within 4-8 hours |

4. **Assemble the crisis team**:
   - Decision maker (CEO/executive with authority to approve statements)
   - Communications lead (PR/comms person drafting and managing messages)
   - Legal counsel (reviewing statements for liability)
   - Technical lead (if product/data related)
   - Customer support lead (frontline response)
   - Designated spokesperson (one voice externally)

5. **Establish the single source of truth**:
   - Create a crisis document with confirmed facts, updated in real-time
   - All external communications must reference this document
   - No one speaks to media without approval from the communications lead

#### Deliverables
- Crisis assessment document: confirmed facts, severity level, stakeholder map, unknowns
- Crisis team assignments with contact information
- Communication priority sequence

#### Exit Conditions
- Severity classified
- Crisis team assembled and briefed
- Facts vs. unknowns clearly separated
- Communication channels identified for each stakeholder group

---

### Phase B2: Holding Statement (1-2 Hours)

#### Purpose
Issue the initial public response. The holding statement buys time for a comprehensive response while demonstrating awareness and accountability.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase B1 complete: assessment done, crisis team assembled
- Decision maker available for approval

#### Key Activities
1. **Draft the holding statement** (4 elements, no more):
   - **Acknowledgment**: We are aware of [issue]
   - **Action**: We are [specific action being taken]
   - **Empathy**: We understand the impact on [affected stakeholders]
   - **Next update**: We will provide an update by [specific time]

2. **Holding statement principles**:
   - Say what you know, not what you guess
   - Never speculate on cause, blame, or scope until confirmed
   - Never say "no comment" -- it implies guilt
   - Do not over-promise on timeline for full response
   - Keep it short: 3-5 sentences maximum
   - Avoid: "We take this seriously" (everyone says this -- it means nothing)

3. **Holding statement template**:

   > We are aware that [brief description of the issue]. Our team is actively [specific action: investigating, resolving, working with authorities]. We understand this affects [stakeholder group] and we are committed to [specific commitment]. We will provide a detailed update by [specific time].
   >
   > For immediate questions, contact [name] at [email/phone].

4. **Distribution sequence**:
   - Status page / service status (if product issue) -- immediate
   - Affected customers (direct communication) -- immediate
   - Social media (where conversation is happening) -- within 30 minutes of approval
   - Media (proactive if Level 3-4; reactive if Level 1-2) -- within 1-2 hours
   - Internal team -- concurrent with external (employees should not learn from the press)

5. **Social media response during holding period**:
   - Respond to direct inquiries with the holding statement language
   - Do not engage with speculation or rumors
   - Do not delete negative comments (unless they contain personal information)
   - Monitor for new information that changes the assessment

#### Deliverables
- Approved holding statement
- Distribution plan: channel, timing, responsible person
- Social media response script for customer-facing teams
- Internal talking points for employees

#### Exit Conditions
- Holding statement approved by decision maker and legal
- Statement distributed across priority channels
- Customer support team briefed with approved language
- **Human review gate**: Every word of the holding statement reviewed and approved by authorized human before release

---

### Phase B3: Full Response (4-8 Hours)

#### Purpose
Issue a comprehensive statement that addresses the situation with transparency, specific actions taken, and commitments to affected stakeholders.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase B2 complete: holding statement issued
- Investigation provides additional confirmed facts
- Legal review completed or in progress

#### Key Activities
1. **Draft the full response** (5-part structure):
   - **What happened**: Clear, factual account of the incident (confirmed facts only)
   - **Who is affected**: Specific scope (number of users, what data, which services)
   - **What we did**: Immediate actions taken to resolve/contain
   - **What we are doing**: Ongoing remediation steps with timeline
   - **What we are changing**: Long-term preventive measures to ensure this does not recur

2. **Tone calibration by crisis type**:

   | Crisis Type | Appropriate Tone | Avoid |
   |---|---|---|
   | Data breach | Serious, transparent, accountable | Minimizing, technical jargon |
   | Product outage | Empathetic, action-oriented | Blame (on vendors, infrastructure) |
   | Executive misconduct | Decisive, values-driven | Defending the individual, deflecting |
   | Safety issue | Urgent, empathetic, precautionary | Legalistic language, delay |
   | Competitive attack | Confident, factual, measured | Emotional, retaliatory |
   | Misinformation | Calm, evidence-based, corrective | Aggressive, dismissive |

3. **Accountability language**:
   - Take ownership: "We made a mistake" (if true) is more powerful than "Mistakes were made"
   - Avoid: passive voice ("the data was compromised"), blame shifting ("our vendor"), minimizing ("a small number of users")
   - Include: specific remediation ("We have hired an independent security firm to audit..."), timeline ("We will complete X by Y date"), compensation (if appropriate)

4. **Legal review requirements**:
   - Every statement reviewed by legal counsel before release
   - Legal review should add protections without stripping humanity from the statement
   - If legal and communications conflict on tone, escalate to decision maker
   - Regulatory notification requirements checked and initiated (GDPR: 72-hour breach notification)

5. **Channel-specific formatting**:
   - **Blog post / press release**: Full statement (500-1000 words)
   - **Email to affected users**: Personalized version with specific impact and remediation steps
   - **Social media**: Summary with link to full statement
   - **Status page**: Technical details with resolution timeline
   - **Media**: Press release or statement to press inquiries

#### Deliverables
- Full response statement approved by decision maker and legal
- Channel-specific versions (email, social, blog, media)
- FAQ document for customer support and media inquiries
- Regulatory notification (if required)

#### Exit Conditions
- Full response distributed to all stakeholder groups
- FAQ published and customer support briefed
- Regulatory notifications sent (if required)
- **Human review gate**: Every external-facing communication reviewed and approved by authorized human and legal counsel

---

### Phase B4: Media Monitoring

#### Purpose
Track real-time coverage, journalist inquiries, and public sentiment during the active crisis period. Information moves fast -- monitoring must be continuous.

#### Entry Conditions
- Phase B3 complete (or concurrent with Phases B2-B3 for Level 3-4 crises)
- Monitoring tools and team in place

#### Key Activities
1. **Real-time monitoring**:
   - Social media: brand mentions, hashtags, sentiment, volume
   - News: Google News, media monitoring tools, journalist inquiries
   - Internal channels: employee questions, customer support ticket volume
   - Monitor every 30 minutes during active crisis (Level 3-4), every 2 hours (Level 1-2)

2. **Journalist inquiry handling**:
   - All media inquiries routed to designated spokesperson only
   - Respond to journalist inquiries within 1 hour during active crisis
   - Never say "no comment" -- provide the approved statement or "We will have more details by [time]"
   - Log every inquiry: journalist, outlet, question, response, timing

3. **Sentiment tracking**:

   | Sentiment Shift | Action |
   |---|---|
   | Sentiment improving after response | Continue monitoring, reduce frequency |
   | Sentiment stable/neutral | Maintain current approach, watch for second-wave triggers |
   | Sentiment worsening despite response | Reassess messaging, consider additional statement or action |
   | New misinformation spreading | Issue factual correction through appropriate channels |
   | Key influencer amplifying negatively | Direct outreach with facts (if relationship exists) |

4. **Escalation triggers** -- situations that require an updated response:
   - New confirmed facts that change the scope or severity
   - Regulatory or legal action announced
   - Major media outlet running a critical story with new information
   - Social media volume exceeding 10x normal mention rate
   - Internal team members making unauthorized public statements

5. **Update cadence**:
   - Provide scheduled updates even when there is no new information ("We are continuing to investigate and will provide another update at [time]")
   - Silence during a crisis is interpreted as hiding -- communicate consistently

#### Deliverables
- Monitoring dashboard: mentions, sentiment, volume, key coverage
- Media inquiry log: journalist, outlet, question, response, timing
- Escalation alerts: any triggers requiring new response

#### Exit Conditions
- Monitoring cadence established and running
- All journalist inquiries responded to within SLA
- Sentiment trend documented
- Crisis team updated with monitoring findings at regular intervals

---

### Phase B5: Post-Crisis Review

#### Purpose
Conduct a thorough post-crisis review to document what happened, evaluate the response, identify improvements, and build organizational resilience for future incidents.

#### Entry Conditions
- Crisis resolved or contained to stable state
- Minimum 1 week since last required public communication
- All team members available for debrief

#### Key Activities
1. **Post-crisis debrief** (within 2 weeks of resolution):
   - Timeline reconstruction: what happened, when, and how did we respond?
   - Response evaluation: what went well? What could we have done better?
   - Communication review: were our statements timely, accurate, and effective?
   - Stakeholder impact: how did customers, employees, media, and public respond?
   - Team performance: was the crisis team effective? Were roles clear?

2. **Metrics assessment**:

   | Metric | Target | Actual |
   |---|---|---|
   | Time to holding statement | <2 hours (Level 3-4) | |
   | Time to full response | <8 hours (Level 3-4) | |
   | Media inquiry response time | <1 hour during active crisis | |
   | Sentiment recovery (to pre-crisis baseline) | <30 days | |
   | Customer churn attributable to crisis | <5% affected users | |
   | Coverage sentiment (positive + neutral) | >60% | |
   | Key message inclusion in coverage | >70% | |

3. **Process improvement**:
   - What would we do differently?
   - What tools or processes were missing?
   - What training is needed?
   - What can be pre-prepared (template statements, contact lists, escalation procedures)?
   - Update the crisis playbook with specific improvements

4. **Crisis playbook updates**:
   - Holding statement templates for common crisis types
   - Pre-approved messaging for known risk scenarios
   - Updated stakeholder contact lists
   - Revised escalation procedures
   - Media training schedule for spokespeople

5. **Organizational resilience building**:
   - Schedule quarterly crisis simulation exercises
   - Annual crisis communication training for spokespeople
   - Semi-annual review of crisis playbook
   - Maintain "dark site" pages ready for rapid deployment

#### Deliverables
- Post-crisis review document: timeline, evaluation, metrics, improvements
- Updated crisis playbook with lessons learned
- Action items with owners and deadlines
- Training recommendations

#### Exit Conditions
- Debrief completed with all crisis team members
- Post-crisis review document finalized and distributed
- Crisis playbook updated with improvements
- Training and simulation schedule confirmed

## Ethics: PR Integrity and Truthfulness

PR operates on trust -- trust between brands and journalists, between organizations and the public. Breaking that trust has permanent consequences.

### Ethical PR Practices

- **Truthfulness**: Every fact in a press release, pitch, or public statement must be verifiable. No exaggeration, no misleading framing.
- **Transparency**: Disclose conflicts of interest, sponsored relationships, and paid placements.
- **Journalist respect**: Respect journalist preferences (no unsolicited calls, no pitching after a "no," no spamming irrelevant stories).
- **Source attribution**: Data and quotes must be accurately attributed and sourced.
- **Embargo integrity**: Honor embargo agreements. Breaking an embargo destroys the relationship and industry reputation.
- **Crisis honesty**: In crisis communication, tell the truth even when it is uncomfortable. Coverups cause more damage than the original incident.
- **AI disclosure**: Disclose AI involvement in content creation when asked. Never claim AI-generated content was written by a named individual.

### Dark Patterns to Avoid

| Pattern | Description | Why It Harms |
|---|---|---|
| Pay-for-play (undisclosed) | Paying for coverage without disclosure | FTC violation; destroys credibility if discovered |
| Fake grassroots (astroturfing) | Creating fake public support or reviews | Deceptive; legal and reputational risk |
| Embellished data | Inflating statistics or cherry-picking | Destroys credibility; journalist blacklisting |
| Spray-and-pray pitching | Mass emailing generic pitches to 500+ journalists | Damages reputation; low ROI; journalist frustration |
| Embargo violations | Breaking agreed-upon embargo timing | Permanent relationship damage; industry blacklisting |
| Ghostwritten quotes | Fabricating quotes attributed to real people without approval | Ethical violation; trust destruction |
| Crisis minimization | Downplaying severity to avoid responsibility | Worsens outcome; legal liability; public backlash |

Before issuing any PR communication, ask: **If this journalist (or the public) found out exactly how this was created and whether every claim is true, would our reputation survive?** If not, revise.

## Response Protocol

When the user requests PR work:

1. **Determine the track**: Is this proactive PR (Track A) or crisis communication (Track B)?
   - If crisis: immediately enter Track B Phase B1. Speed matters.
   - If proactive: proceed through Track A phases sequentially.

2. **Read the strongest available context** (Pre-Flight): brand and SOSTAC first when available.

3. **Ask the diagnostic questions** for the relevant track.

4. **Identify the entry phase**:
   - New PR campaign: Track A, Phase A1
   - Media list needed: Track A, Phase A2
   - Press release or pitch needed: Track A, Phase A3
   - Active crisis: Track B, Phase B1 (immediately)
   - Post-crisis debrief: Track B, Phase B5

5. **Execute the relevant phases** sequentially, producing deliverables at each phase.

6. **Enforce the human review gate** at every phase that produces external-facing communication.

7. **Save deliverables** to the resolved path (see Path Resolution).

8. **Recommend next steps**: what to send, when to follow up, what to monitor.

### When to escalate

- SEO and link building beyond earned coverage -> route to paw-mkt-seo
- Influencer or creator partnerships -> route to paw-mkt-influencer
- Social media amplification strategy -> route to paw-mkt-social
- Long-form content (blog, case studies) -> route to paw-mkt-content
- Email nurture for media relationships -> route to paw-mkt-email
- Legal review of any public statement -> recommend legal counsel (mandatory for Level 3-4 crises)
